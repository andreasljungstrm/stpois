\begin{abstract}
\noindent
When every covariate of a generalized linear model is categorical, the
log likelihood collapses onto within-cell aggregates of the
cross-classification, and estimation on the $J$ cells reproduces the
microdata maximum-likelihood estimator at a fraction of the cost.  A
single continuous covariate destroys this collapse: we show that
computing the likelihood then requires, in effect, the entire
within-cell empirical distribution of the continuous covariates, so no
fixed-dimensional cell summary suffices.  The contribution of this paper
is that a moving summary does: for exponential dispersion families, the
exact score vector and Fisher information matrix at any parameter value
are linear combinations of three low-order moments of an
\emph{exponentially tilted} within-cell measure---for the Poisson
family, an Esscher transform of the within-cell exposure measure.
Consequently every Newton or Fisher-scoring iteration is computable from
one streaming pass over the microdata at $O(Np_c^2)$ cost, with $p_c$
the number of continuous covariates only, followed by algebra on the
cells; the resulting iterates are algebraically \emph{identical} to
full-design iterations, not approximations to them.  We give the
streaming algorithm, its complexity and numerical properties, and exact
cell-accumulated robust and cluster-robust covariance estimators.
Experiments verify iterate-for-iterate agreement with dense Newton at
$10^{-13}$--$10^{-15}$ across Poisson, logistic, and gamma models while
running one to two orders of magnitude faster than dense and
general-purpose implementations and several times faster than a
sparse-design IRLS, with exactness unaffected by severe cell imbalance
or rare events.  Two applications---a logistic on-time-performance model
for the 336{,}776 flights of the public \texttt{nycflights13} data, and
a register-scale synthetic epidemiology study with five million
person-year episodes and $10{,}000$ absorbed municipality--year
effects---fit with inference in seconds.  An online supplement develops
companion theory: global convergence and sharp local rates for the
nonlinear alternating-projections absorption of high-dimensional fixed
effects, asymptotics up to the proportional regime $J/N \to c$, and
Firth, penalized, and conditional-likelihood extensions.
\end{abstract}

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{Keywords:} generalized linear models; exponential
tilting; sufficient statistics; streaming algorithms; high-dimensional
fixed effects; register data.

\section{Introduction}\label{sec:intro}
% =========================================================================

A large share of applied statistical work now takes place on data sets
whose size is set not by a sampling budget but by an administrative
process: full-population registers, insurance claim files, electronic
health records, transportation and digital trace data.  The models
fitted to such data are rarely exotic---Poisson regression for event
rates, logistic regression for binary outcomes, gamma regression for
costs---but their covariate structure has a characteristic shape.  A
small number of continuous covariates of scientific interest (income,
exposure, a polygenic score) appear alongside a rich categorical
apparatus: demographic strata such as age band $\times$ sex $\times$
education, operational strata such as carrier $\times$ route $\times$
month, and often fixed-effect factors with thousands of levels.  The
cross-classification of the categorical covariates partitions the $N$
observations into $J$ cells, and it is the interplay between $N$, $J$,
and the continuous covariates that determines whether
maximum-likelihood estimation is an interactive computation or an
overnight batch job.

Two classical observations frame the problem.  First, when \emph{every}
covariate is categorical, the exponential-family log likelihood depends
on the data only through within-cell aggregates; estimation on the $J$
collapsed cells reproduces the microdata maximum-likelihood estimator
(MLE) exactly, and the per-iteration cost drops from $O(Nq^2)$ to
$O(Jq^2)$ for $q$ parameters.  This is the standard device behind
register-based rate modeling in epidemiology and demography
\citep{holford1980,laird1981,clayton1993}.  Second, the collapse is
fragile: a single continuous covariate destroys it, because the cumulant
function of the family is strictly convex, so cell sums of nonlinear
transforms of the linear predictor cannot be recovered from cell means.
Analysts then face an unattractive choice between discretizing the
continuous covariates---introducing bias that is hard to quantify---and
abandoning the cell structure entirely, paying the full $O(Nq^2)$ price
per iteration even though most of the $q$ parameters are attached to
covariates that are constant within cells.  General-purpose GLM
software pays this price by default; software for high-dimensional
fixed effects \citep{guimaraes2010,gaure2013,correia2020,berge2018,
stammann2018} removes \emph{dummy-coded factors} from the design by
demeaning, but continuous-by-cell interplay of the kind above is not a
demeaning problem, and the cell structure of the categorical part goes
unexploited.

This paper develops a resolution that is exact rather than approximate.
The starting point is a precise statement of what breaks
(Proposition~\ref{prop:sufficiency}): with continuous covariates, any
statistic from which the likelihood \emph{function} can be evaluated
must determine the entire within-cell empirical distribution of the
continuous covariates---the within-cell Laplace transform appears in the
likelihood, and Laplace transforms determine measures.  No fixed cell
summary can therefore support the full likelihood surface.  The
contribution is that estimation does not need the full surface; it needs
score and curvature \emph{at the current iterate}, and these have an
exact finite representation.  Theorem~\ref{thm:tilted} shows that for
exponential dispersion families with canonical link, the score vector
and Fisher information at any parameter value are linear combinations of
the zeroth-, first-, and second-order moments of an \emph{exponentially
tilted probability measure} on each cell---for the Poisson family, an
Esscher transform of the within-cell empirical exposure measure,
connecting the construction to classical tilting ideas in saddlepoint
approximation and empirical likelihood
\citep{esscher1932,daniels1954,efron1981,jensen1995,kitamura1997}.  The
tilt is the right \emph{dynamic} object: it changes with the iterate,
which is exactly why a fixed collapse fails and a per-iteration one
succeeds.  Corollary~\ref{cor:complexity} converts the identity into
complexity: each Newton iteration costs one streaming $O(N(p^2+p))$
pass over the microdata---$p$ the number of continuous columns
only---plus $O(J(k^2+kp))$ cell algebra, against $O(N(p+k)^2)$ for the
full design, a saving that grows quadratically in the richness $k$ of
the categorical structure.  Because the identity is exact, the
accelerated iterates are \emph{identical} to full-design Newton---not
close, identical---which Section~\ref{sec:sim-validation} verifies at
the $10^{-13}$--$10^{-15}$ level, iterate by iterate, across Poisson,
logistic, and gamma models.  Fisher scoring under non-canonical links
has the same structure (Proposition~\ref{prop:noncanonical}); for the
gamma--log family the tilt weights are identically one and the moments
are constant across iterations.

The second half of the paper is deliberately computational.
Section~\ref{sec:algorithms} gives the streaming algorithm and its
implementation envelope: numerical safeguards (step-halving,
log-domain evaluation, rank-deficiency fallbacks), memory layout and
parallelization of the bandwidth-bound accumulation pass, exact
cell-accumulated robust and cluster-robust covariance estimators, and
the combination with absorbed fixed-effect factors, for which the
companion theory---global convergence of the nonlinear
alternating-projections cycle, its local rates for two and for three or
more factors, and high-dimensional asymptotics up to one fixed effect
per two observations---is developed in the online supplement.
Section~\ref{sec:simulations} contains the experiments: iterate-level
exactness and per-iteration cost against dense Newton
(Section~\ref{sec:sim-validation}); total-time benchmarks against
\texttt{statsmodels} (the standard general-purpose Python GLM) and
against a sparse-design IRLS, the natural ``just use sparse matrices''
alternative (Section~\ref{sec:sim-baselines}); and sensitivity to
severe cell imbalance and rare events
(Section~\ref{sec:sim-sensitivity}).  Section~\ref{sec:applications}
applies the method twice: to the public \texttt{nycflights13} data
(a logistic on-time-performance model with a
carrier--origin--destination--month cell structure, verified
coefficient-for-coefficient against \texttt{statsmodels} at a tenth of
its cost), and to a fully reproducible register-scale synthetic study
(five million person-year episodes, $10{,}000$ absorbed
municipality--year effects, cluster-robust inference, seconds on one
core).  Section~\ref{sec:discussion} summarizes and points to the
supplement's theory.  Proofs of the main-paper results are in the
appendix; every number regenerates from short scripts included with the
manuscript.

Relative to the literature, the closest computational ideas are the
collapsed sufficient statistics of classical log-linear rate modeling
\citep{holford1980,clayton1993}, which the tilted moments generalize
from ``all categorical'' to ``categorical plus continuous''; and the
alternating-projections HDFE stack
\citep{guimaraes2010,gaure2013,correia2016,correia2020,berge2018},
which removes dummy factors but streams the full non-absorbed design
through every IRLS step---the two devices are complementary, and
Section~\ref{sec:absorb-impl} combines them.  The framework is
implemented in the Stata command \texttt{stpois} for the Poisson
event-history case and in an open-source Python reference
implementation used for all experiments here.

\section{Exponential family models and the failure of ordinary
collapse}\label{sec:tilted}
% =========================================================================

\subsection{Model and notation}\label{sec:model}

Observations $i = 1, \dots, N$ carry a response $y_i$, a vector
$\bx_i \in \R^{p}$ of \emph{individual-level} covariates (those involving
at least one continuous variable, including continuous--categorical
interaction columns), a vector $\bw_{j(i)} \in \R^{k}$ of
\emph{cell-level} covariates, and a known offset $o_i$.  The map
$j : \{1,\dots,N\} \to \{1,\dots,J\}$ assigns each observation to the
cell of the cross-classification of the categorical covariates;
$\bw_j$ is constant within cell $j$ by construction.  Conditional on the
covariates, responses are independent draws from an exponential
dispersion family
\begin{equation}\label{eq:edf}
f(y_i \mid \theta_i, \phi)
  = \exp\!\left\{ \frac{y_i \theta_i - b(\theta_i)}{\phi}
      + c(y_i, \phi) \right\},
\end{equation}
with $b : \Theta \to \R$ strictly convex and three times continuously
differentiable on the open canonical parameter space $\Theta \subseteq \R$,
and dispersion $\phi > 0$ \citep{mccullagh1989}.  The mean and variance
are $\mu_i = b'(\theta_i)$ and $\Var(y_i) = \phi\, b''(\theta_i)$.  We
model the linear predictor
\begin{equation}\label{eq:linpred}
\eta_i = \bgamma' \bx_i + \bdelta' \bw_{j(i)} + o_i,
\qquad \btheta = (\bgamma', \bdelta')' \in \R^{q}, \quad q = p + k,
\end{equation}
and, until Section~\ref{sec:noncanonical}, take the link to be canonical,
$\theta_i = \eta_i$.  The Poisson event-history model that motivates much
of the applied work is the special case $b(\theta) = e^{\theta}$,
$\phi = 1$, $y_i \in \{0, 1, 2, \dots\}$ an event count, and
$o_i = \log t_i$ the log of the time at risk; the piecewise-exponential
equivalence of \citet{holford1980,laird1981,whitehead1980} makes this the
canonical likelihood for register-based survival analysis.  Up to terms
not involving $\btheta$, the log likelihood is
\begin{equation}\label{eq:loglik}
\ell(\btheta)
  = \frac{1}{\phi} \sum_{i=1}^{N}
      \left\{ y_i \eta_i - b(\eta_i) \right\}.
\end{equation}
Because \eqref{eq:loglik} is a sum of concave functions of the linear
predictor, $\ell$ is concave on $\R^q$, strictly so on the quotient of
$\R^q$ by the null space of the stacked design
$\ba_i = (\bx_i', \bw_{j(i)}')'$.  We write
$\bU(\btheta) = \nabla \ell$ for the score,
$\bH(\btheta) = -\nabla^2 \ell$ for the negative Hessian, and note that
for canonical links $\bH$ equals the Fisher information
$\bF(\btheta) = \E[-\nabla^2 \ell]$, so Newton--Raphson and Fisher scoring
coincide.

\subsection{Cell collapse and its failure}\label{sec:sufficiency}

Suppose first that $p = 0$: every covariate is categorical, so
$\eta_i = \bdelta'\bw_j + o_i$ varies over $i$ within a cell only through
the offset.  For the Poisson family with $o_i = \log t_i$,
\eqref{eq:loglik} becomes
$\ell(\bdelta) = \sum_j \{ D_j\, \bdelta'\bw_j - T_j e^{\bdelta'\bw_j} \}
+ \mathrm{const}$, with $D_j = \sum_{i \in j} y_i$ and
$T_j = \sum_{i \in j} t_i$: the collapsed cell data $(D_j, T_j)$ carry all
the information, and estimation on the $J$ cells is exact.  The following
proposition records why no analogous finite collapse survives the
introduction of a continuous covariate, and in what precise sense.  Define
the within-cell \emph{exposure measure}
$\nu_j = \sum_{i \in j} e^{o_i} \delta_{\bx_i}$ on $\R^p$ (for Poisson,
$\nu_j = \sum_{i \in j} t_i \delta_{\bx_i}$), a finite discrete measure
recording the joint configuration of exposures and continuous covariates
in cell $j$.

\begin{proposition}[Collapse and its failure]\label{prop:sufficiency}
Consider the Poisson family with offsets.  (i) If $\bx_i = \bar\bx_j$ for
all $i \in j$ and all $j$, the log likelihood \eqref{eq:loglik} depends on
the data only through $\{(D_j, T_j, \bar\bx_j, \sum_{i\in j} y_i \bx_i)\}_{j \le J}$,
and the MLE on the collapsed cells equals the microdata MLE.
(ii) In general, $\ell(\bgamma, \bdelta)$ depends on the data through
$\{ S_j, D_j, \mathcal{L}_{\nu_j} \}_{j\le J}$, where
$S_j = \sum_{i \in j} y_i \bx_i$ and
$\mathcal{L}_{\nu_j}(\bgamma) = \int e^{\bgamma'\bx} \nu_j(\dd\bx)
= \sum_{i \in j} t_i e^{\bgamma'\bx_i}$ is the Laplace transform of
$\nu_j$; and $\mathcal{L}_{\nu_j}$ determines $\nu_j$.  Consequently any
statistic from which the likelihood function can be computed for all
$\bgamma$ in an open set must determine the entire within-cell exposure
measure, whose support size---hence the dimension of any such
statistic---grows with the number of distinct covariate values in the
cell.  (iii) By strict convexity of the exponential (Jensen),
$\mathcal{L}_{\nu_j}(\bgamma) \ge T_j e^{\bgamma' \bar\bx_j}$ with
equality for some $\bgamma \ne \bzero$ direction only if $\bx$ is
$\nu_j$-a.s.\ constant along that direction: replacing microdata by cell
means strictly understates the within-cell mass of the cumulant term and
is not a reparametrization of the same likelihood.
\end{proposition}

Part (ii) is the formal statement of the practical dictum that
``continuous covariates destroy collapsibility'': the fixed-dimensional
sufficient statistic $\sum_i y_i \ba_i$ for the canonical parameter still
exists, but \emph{computing the likelihood}---and hence the MLE---requires
functionals of the data whose dimension is of order $N$, not $J$.  The
next subsection shows that the required functionals nevertheless have a
special structure: for each trial value of $\bgamma$ they are three
low-order moments of a tilted measure, computable in one linear pass.

\section{Tilted cell moments}\label{sec:tilted-main}
% =========================================================================

\subsection{Exponentially tilted cell moments}\label{sec:tilted-moments}

\begin{definition}[Tilted within-cell measures]\label{def:tilt}
Fix $\btheta = (\bgamma, \bdelta)$ and let $\omega_i =
b''(\eta_i) > 0$ be the information weight of observation $i$.  The
\emph{information-tilted measure} on cell $j$ is the probability measure
\[
P_{j,\btheta}(\{i\}) = \frac{\omega_i}{\sum_{i' \in j} \omega_{i'}},
\qquad i \in j .
\]
For the Poisson family, $\omega_i = \mu_i = t_i
e^{\bdelta'\bw_j + \bgamma'\bx_i}$, the factor $e^{\bdelta'\bw_j}$
cancels, and $P_{j,\btheta}$ reduces to the \emph{Esscher transform}
(exponential tilt) of the normalized exposure measure $\nu_j / T_j$:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:esscher}
P_{j,\bgamma}(\dd\bx)
 = \frac{e^{\bgamma'\bx}\,\nu_j(\dd\bx)}{\mathcal{L}_{\nu_j}(\bgamma)},
\end{equation}
free of $\bdelta$.  Its moments are derivatives of the within-cell
cumulant generating function: with
$K_j(\bgamma) = \log \mathcal{L}_{\nu_j}(\bgamma)$,
$\E_{P_{j,\bgamma}}[\bx] = \nabla K_j(\bgamma)$ and
$\Var_{P_{j,\bgamma}}(\bx) = \nabla^2 K_j(\bgamma)$.
\end{definition}

The tilt \eqref{eq:esscher} is precisely the change of measure that
underlies saddlepoint approximation \citep{daniels1954,jensen1995} and
exponential-tilting estimators in the empirical-likelihood family
\citep{efron1981,kitamura1997,schennach2007}; here it appears not as an
approximation device but as an exact algebraic identity for the score and
information, as follows.  Write, for each cell $j$,
\begin{equation}\label{eq:moments}
M_j^{(0)} = \sum_{i \in j} \omega_i, \qquad
\mathbf{m}_j = \sum_{i \in j} \omega_i \bx_i, \qquad
\bQ = \sum_{i=1}^N \omega_i \bx_i \bx_i' ,
\end{equation}
and let $B_j = \sum_{i \in j} b'(\eta_i)$ and
$R_j = \sum_{i \in j} (y_i - b'(\eta_i))$ denote the cell sums of means
and of raw residuals.

\begin{theorem}[Exact tilted-moment representation]\label{thm:tilted}
For the model \eqref{eq:edf}--\eqref{eq:linpred} with canonical link, at
every $\btheta \in \R^q$:
\begin{enumerate}
\item[(i)] The score is
\[
\phi\, \bU(\btheta) =
\begin{pmatrix}
\sum_{i} y_i \bx_i - \sum_{j} \mathbf{m}^{b'}_j \\[2pt]
\sum_{j} \bw_j R_j
\end{pmatrix},
\qquad
\mathbf{m}^{b'}_j = \sum_{i \in j} b'(\eta_i)\,\bx_i
  = B_j\, \E_{P^{b'}_{j,\btheta}}[\bx],
\]
where $P^{b'}_{j,\btheta}$ is the tilt of Definition~\ref{def:tilt} with
$b'$ in place of $b''$.
\item[(ii)] The information matrix is
\[
\phi\, \bF(\btheta) =
\begin{pmatrix}
\bQ & \sum_j \mathbf{m}_j \bw_j' \\[2pt]
\sum_j \bw_j \mathbf{m}_j' & \sum_j M_j^{(0)} \bw_j \bw_j'
\end{pmatrix},
\qquad
\mathbf{m}_j = M_j^{(0)}\, \E_{P_{j,\btheta}}[\bx],
\quad
\bQ = \sum_j M_j^{(0)}\, \E_{P_{j,\btheta}}[\bx\bx'] .
\]
\item[(iii)] For the Poisson family, $b' = b''$, the two tilts coincide
with the $\bdelta$-free Esscher transform \eqref{eq:esscher}, and
$B_j = M_j^{(0)} = e^{\bdelta'\bw_j} \mathcal{L}_{\nu_j}(\bgamma)$,
$\mathbf{m}_j = M_j^{(0)} \nabla K_j(\bgamma)$,
and the $\bgamma$-block of $\bF$ is
$\sum_j M_j^{(0)} \{ \nabla^2 K_j(\bgamma) + \nabla K_j \nabla K_j' \}$.
\item[(iv)] The Newton step
$\btheta^{+} = \btheta + \bF(\btheta)^{-1} \bU(\btheta)$ computed from the
quantities in (i)--(ii) is identical to the Newton step computed from the
full $N \times q$ design.
\end{enumerate}
\end{theorem}

Claim (iv) is trivial once (i)--(ii) are established---the two
computations produce the same matrices---but it is the operative point:
the decomposition involves \emph{no linearization, discretization, or
approximation of any kind}, so the accelerated algorithm is not a fast
approximate estimator but a fast exact organization of the arithmetic of
the MLE.  Section~\ref{sec:sim-validation} exhibits agreement of the full
iterate \emph{sequences} at the $10^{-13}$ level, which is the accumulation
of floating-point reordering only.

Statistically, the representation has content beyond bookkeeping.  The
$\bdelta$-score in (i) compares each cell's observed event mass with its
expected mass under the current tilt; the cross-information in (ii) is
the tilted covariance between cell-level and individual-level design; and
the $\bgamma$-block in (iii) decomposes into a within-cell tilted
covariance $\nabla^2 K_j$ plus a between-cell term.  In particular the
identification of $\bgamma$ rests exactly on
$\sum_j M_j^{(0)} \nabla^2 K_j(\bgamma) \succ \bzero$: continuous
covariates are identified through their \emph{within-cell, tilt-weighted}
variation---a fact that the block structure displays, and that drives
the Schur-complement profile Hessians used throughout the companion
theory in the online supplement.  (When a continuous covariate is
constant within cells---a route-level distance, say---it remains
identified through between-cell variation as long as the cell-level
design $\bw_j$ is not saturated; Section~\ref{sec:app-flights}
exercises this case.)

\begin{corollary}[Per-iteration complexity]\label{cor:complexity}
One Newton iteration computed via Theorem~\ref{thm:tilted} costs
\[
\underbrace{O\!\left(N(p^2 + p + 1)\right)}_{\text{microdata pass}}
+ \underbrace{O\!\left(J(k^2 + kp)\right)}_{\text{cell assembly}}
+ \underbrace{O\!\left((p+k)^3\right)}_{\text{solve}}
\]
floating-point operations and one $O(N)$ memory sweep, versus
$O\!\left(N(p+k)^2\right) + O\!\left((p+k)^3\right)$ for the full-design
iteration.  When $N \gg J(p+k)$ the leading-order saving is the factor
$(p+k)^2 / (p^2+p+1)$, which grows quadratically in the richness $k$ of
the categorical structure; when additionally $p = 0$ the microdata pass
degenerates to the classical cell collapse and the entire iteration runs
at $O(Jk^2)$.
\end{corollary}

Two remarks on the constant factors.  First, the microdata pass is a
fused stream: one evaluation of $b'(\eta_i)$, $b''(\eta_i)$ per
observation, $p+1$ multiply--accumulate reductions into cell bins, and a
rank-one update of the $p \times p$ Gram matrix; on modern hardware it is
memory-bandwidth-bound, so measured speedups (Section~\ref{sec:sim-validation})
are large but smaller than the FLOP ratio.  Second, the accumulations are
plain segmented sums---\texttt{bincount} operations---so they
parallelize and stream trivially; no $N \times q$ design matrix is ever
formed, which converts a memory-infeasible problem
(Section~\ref{sec:app-register}) into an in-cache one.

\subsection{Non-canonical links}\label{sec:noncanonical}

For a non-canonical, three-times-differentiable, strictly monotone link
$g$ with $\mu_i = g^{-1}(\eta_i)$, define the usual Fisher weight and
working residual
\[
\omega_i = \frac{1}{V(\mu_i)\, g'(\mu_i)^2}, \qquad
r_i = (y_i - \mu_i)\, g'(\mu_i)\,\omega_i ,
\]
with $V$ the variance function.  Fisher scoring replaces
$(b'(\eta_i), b''(\eta_i))$ by $(\mu_i, \omega_i)$ and the raw residual
by $r_i$.

\begin{proposition}[Tilted Fisher scoring]\label{prop:noncanonical}
With $\omega_i$ and $r_i$ as above, the expected-information scoring step
admits the representation of Theorem~\ref{thm:tilted} with
$M_j^{(0)}, \mathbf{m}_j, \bQ$ built from $\omega_i$ and with $R_j =
\sum_{i \in j} r_i$; the fixed points of the resulting iteration are
exactly the roots of the ML score equations, and
Corollary~\ref{cor:complexity} applies verbatim.  For the gamma family
with log link, $V(\mu) = \mu^2$ and $g'(\mu) = 1/\mu$ give
$\omega_i \equiv 1$: the tilted moments reduce to \emph{untilted} cell
moments of the covariates, fixed across iterations, so after the first
pass each subsequent iteration costs $O(N p) + O(Jk^2)$ only.
\end{proposition}


\subsection{Families in scope}\label{sec:families-main}

Three instances cover most applied use and are carried through the
experiments.  \emph{Poisson with log link} (canonical): the tilt is the
$\bdelta$-free Esscher transform \eqref{eq:esscher}, and the
event-history offset $o_i = \log t_i$ makes this the estimator of
piecewise-constant hazard models on episode-split survival data
\citep{holford1980,whitehead1980}.  \emph{Logistic} (canonical): the
tilt weights $\omega_i = \mu_i(1-\mu_i)$ concentrate the within-cell
measure on observations near the decision boundary and depend on
$\bdelta$, so the moments are re-accumulated each iteration---which the
single pass does anyway.  \emph{Gamma with log link} (non-canonical,
Proposition~\ref{prop:noncanonical}): the Fisher weights are identically
one, the moments are constant across iterations, and after the first
pass each iteration costs $O(Np) + O(Jk^2)$.  We deliberately stop the
catalogue here; the point is the structure, not an enumeration.

\section{Algorithms and implementation}\label{sec:algorithms}
% =========================================================================

\subsection{The streaming pass}\label{sec:streaming}

Algorithm~\ref{alg:tilted} states the method operationally.  Its
preprocessing is one factorization of the categorical covariates into
cell identifiers $j(i)$ and one construction of the $J \times k$
cell-level design $\bW$; both are $O(N)$ given a sort or hash of the
categorical columns, and both are shared across models on the same
cells.

\begin{algorithm}[t]
\caption{Tilted-moment Newton / Fisher scoring}\label{alg:tilted}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\State Precompute cell ids $j(i)$, cell design $\bW \in \R^{J \times k}$,
  continuous columns $\bX \in \R^{N \times p}$, offsets $o_i$;
  initialize $\btheta_0 = (\bgamma_0, \bdelta_0)$.
\Repeat
  \State \emph{Stream over $i = 1, \dots, N$:} compute
    $\eta_i = \bgamma'\bx_i + (\bW\bdelta)_{j(i)} + o_i$, the weight
    $\omega_i$, and the residual $r_i$; accumulate
    $M^{(0)}_{j(i)} \mathrel{+}= \omega_i$;\;
    $\mathbf{m}_{j(i)} \mathrel{+}= \omega_i \bx_i$;\;
    $R_{j(i)} \mathrel{+}= r_i$;\;
    $\bU_\gamma \mathrel{+}= r_i \bx_i$;\;
    $\bQ \mathrel{+}= \omega_i \bx_i \bx_i'$.
  \State \emph{Cell assembly:} $\bU_\delta = \bW'\mathbf{R}$;\;
    $\bF_{\gamma\delta} = \mathbf{m}'\bW$;\;
    $\bF_{\delta\delta} = \bW'\diag(M^{(0)})\bW$;\;
    $\bF_{\gamma\gamma} = \bQ$.
  \State Solve the $(p{+}k)$-dimensional system
    $\bF \Delta = \bU$; update
    $\btheta \gets \btheta + \lambda \Delta$ with backtracking
    $\lambda \in \{1, \tfrac12, \dots\}$ on the log likelihood.
\Until{$\max_\ell |\Delta\theta_\ell|/(1 + |\theta_\ell|) < \tau$}
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

By Theorem~\ref{thm:tilted}(iv) the iterate sequence is identical to
full-design Newton from the same start; every design decision below
concerns constants, not correctness.

\subsection{Numerical safeguards}\label{sec:stability}

Three matter in practice.  (i) \emph{Monotonicity}: the backtracking
line search on the log likelihood guarantees ascent and terminates,
since the Newton direction of a positive-definite system is an ascent
direction; in well-conditioned problems the full step is accepted after
the first iterations and convergence is quadratic.  (ii) \emph{Log-domain
evaluation}: likelihood and mean evaluations use \texttt{log1p} /
\texttt{logaddexp}-style kernels so that extreme linear predictors
(produced, e.g., en route to a separated fit) yield $-\infty$ log
likelihoods rather than overflow.  (iii) \emph{Rank deficiency}: if the
assembled $\bF$ is singular---collinear cell-level columns, or vanishing
weights under quasi-separation---the solve falls back to a minimum-norm
(pseudoinverse) direction and the iteration runs to its cap with the
non-convergence flagged, never silently; separation is a property of the
data, not of the algorithm, and Section~S3 of the supplement develops
the Firth-adjusted variant that restores a finite maximizer inside the
same pass.

\subsection{Memory layout and parallelism}\label{sec:memory}

The gap between measured and FLOP ratios merits a constructive comment,
since closing it is an implementation exercise with a known playbook.
The tilted pass streams $O(Np)$ numbers and performs $O(Np^2)$ flops, so
for small $p$ its arithmetic intensity is low and the memory bus, not
the ALU, sets the speed; the dense baseline, by contrast, is a BLAS-3
Gram update running near peak.  Three standard remedies raise the tilted
pass toward bandwidth saturation and beyond: (i) \emph{cache locality}:
storing the microdata sorted by cell converts the binned accumulations
into contiguous segmented sums, so each cell's partial moments stay in
registers/L1 and the pass touches each cache line once (this is how the
Mata implementation in \texttt{stpois} organizes the sweep, following
\citealp{correia2016}); (ii) \emph{fusion}: the weight evaluation,
residual, binned sums, and Gram update share one loop, so the data are
read once per iteration rather than once per moment; and (iii)
\emph{parallelism}: cells are independent, so the pass shards trivially
across threads with per-thread partial moments and a $J k$-sized
reduction, with no atomics on the hot path---the same structure GPU
segmented-reduction primitives expect.  The single-threaded NumPy
reference implementation uses none of (i)--(iii) beyond what
\texttt{bincount} provides, so the reported $12\times$--$117\times$
should be read as a floor; the FLOP ratio governs how the comparison
scales with $(p, k)$, and the bandwidth analysis governs the attainable
constant.

\subsection{Robust and clustered variance estimation}\label{sec:vce}

All variance estimators used in practice share the cell structure.  The
observed/expected information is $\bF$ itself (Theorem~\ref{thm:tilted}).
The HC-type sandwich needs $\sum_i \hat u_i^2 \ba_i \ba_i'$ with
$\hat u_i = y_i - \hat\mu_i$; its blocks accumulate cellwise exactly like
\eqref{eq:moments} with $\omega_i$ replaced by $\hat u_i^2$.  The
cluster-robust estimator sums scores within clusters before the outer
product; when clusters are unions of cells (the common design: cluster on
region, cells within region) the accumulation is again one pass.  These
identities are what make \emph{inference}, not just point estimation,
run at cell-collapsed cost; they are implemented in the reference code
and validated against dense computation in the test suite.



\subsection{Absorbed fixed-effect factors}\label{sec:absorb-impl}

Applications often add factors whose level counts preclude even
cell-level treatment---firms, hospitals, municipality--year
interactions.  The framework composes with the standard absorption
device: augment the linear predictor with $G$ absorbed factors
$\alpha^{(g)}_{g(i)}$, concentrate them out by cycling closed-form
profile updates (for Poisson, $\alpha^{(g)}_a = \log\{\sum_{i: g(i)=a}
y_i / \sum_{i: g(i)=a} \exp(\eta_i - \alpha^{(g)}_a)\}$, the
multiplicative update of \citealp{berge2018,correia2020}), and take each
tilted-moment Newton step on $(\bgamma, \bdelta)$ at the profiled
effects, with the inner cycle re-solved at every trial point of the line
search (Algorithm~S1 of the supplement).  Each inner sweep is $O(N)$;
with a single absorbed factor the inner solve is exact in one sweep.

The theory of this composition is the subject of the online supplement,
and we summarize what is proved there.  Theorem~S1 establishes
\emph{global convergence} of the nonlinear cycle to the MLE whenever the
MLE exists, for canonical and more generally log-concave links---a
guarantee previously absent from the applied HDFE--GLM literature.
Theorems~S2--S3 give the \emph{local rate}: for two absorbed factors the
per-sweep contraction equals $\cos^2$ of the Friedrichs angle between
the weighted factor subspaces---equivalently the squared second singular
value of a normalized weighted cross-tabulation---and for $G \ge 3$
factors the exact rate is the spectral radius of a level-space sweep
operator determined by the pairwise cross-tabulations alone, computable
by power iteration before touching the microdata, with an angle-based
product certificate; observed contraction factors match these
predictions to three decimals.  Theorems~S4--S6 give asymptotics for the
estimator (which is the MLE, so classical theory applies at fixed
dimension) up to the proportional regime of one Poisson fixed effect per
bounded-size cell, $J/N \to c \in (0,1)$, where profiling coincides with
conditioning and inference is unbiased with valid cell-clustered
standard errors.  Separated absorbed groups (no events) are detected and
dropped a priori, exactly as in \citet{correia2020};
Section~\ref{sec:app-register} exercises the full composition at scale.

\subsection{Sensitivity: imbalance and rare events}
\label{sec:sim-sensitivity}

Study 1c re-runs the exactness comparison under stress: cell sizes drawn
from a Zipf($1.6$) law (a few giant cells, a long tail of tiny ones) and
event rates pushed to $\sim 0.2\%$ (Poisson means $\sim 0.002$; logistic
prevalence $\sim 0.5\%$), with event-free cells dropped as the MLE
requires.  Across all eight family $\times$ scenario combinations the
maximum deviation from dense Newton remains at
$10^{-15}$--$10^{-14}$, convergence takes at most $12$ iterations
(rare-event fits need a few more steps, as expected from their flatter
likelihoods), and the streaming pass time is unchanged---the
accumulation is a data-independent $O(N)$ sweep, so imbalance shifts
work between cells without changing its total.  Exactness is an
algebraic identity and is expected to survive stress; the value of the
study is confirming that the \emph{implementation} (binned accumulation,
line search, drop rules) inherits the robustness
(script \texttt{sim1b\_baselines.py}).

\section{Applications}\label{sec:applications}
% =========================================================================

\subsection{On-time performance of NYC flights}\label{sec:app-flights}

The first application is to public data, chosen so that every number can
be reproduced from a fresh download: the \texttt{nycflights13} data set
\citep{wickham2018}---all $336{,}776$ flights departing New York City's
three airports in 2013, from the U.S.\ Bureau of Transportation
Statistics.  We model the probability of a late arrival (arrival delay
$\ge 15$ minutes, the FAA on-time criterion; $24.5\%$ of the $327{,}346$
flights with an observed delay) by logistic regression with a cell
structure of carrier $\times$ origin $\times$ destination $\times$
month---$J = 3{,}863$ observed combinations, entering through a
main-effects cell-level design with $k = 131$ parameters ($16$ carriers,
$3$ origins, $103$ destinations, $12$ months)---and $p = 2$ continuous
covariates: scheduled departure time (in hours, centered) and log
distance (standardized).  One destination (LEX, a single on-time
flight) is quasi-separated---its dummy's MLE is $-\infty$---and is
dropped by the level-wise separation rule, after which the MLE exists.

The tilted logistic fit converges in $7$ Newton iterations,
$1.7$ seconds including robust standard errors.  The diurnal effect is
the substantive headline: the odds of a late arrival multiply by
$1.11$ per hour of scheduled departure time (robust SE on the log scale
$0.001$)---delays cascade through the day, a finding familiar from the
air-transportation literature, here estimated jointly with a full
operational cell structure.  Log distance illustrates the boundary case
flagged in Section~\ref{sec:tilted-main}: distance is constant within
origin--destination routes, hence within cells, so after destination
main effects it is identified only through cross-route contrasts and is
correspondingly imprecise ($+0.65$, robust SE $0.28$)---the estimator
handles cell-constant continuous covariates correctly and the standard
error says exactly how little such a covariate adds.

As external validation, the identical model was fitted with
\texttt{statsmodels} GLM on the dense $327{,}345 \times 133$ design
($0.35$\,GB): maximum absolute coefficient deviation
$2.3 \times 10^{-13}$, at $16.6$ seconds versus $1.7$---the same
estimator, ten times cheaper, with no design matrix in memory
(script \texttt{app\_flights.py}; the data file is fetched from the
public repository cited in the script).

\subsection{A register-scale stress test}\label{sec:app-register}

The second application is a stress test at the scale that motivates the
framework:
spatial epidemiology on an emulated population register.  Because
genuine Nordic register microdata cannot leave certified secure
environments, the study uses a fully reproducible synthetic register,
calibrated to published all-cause mortality gradients (Gompertz age
slope, female advantage of $\sim\!0.45$ on the log scale, educational
gradients of $0.1$--$0.4$, a secular decline of $1.2\%$ per year); the
generator is 40 lines of the supplement and every number below
regenerates end to end in under a minute.  The scientific template is a
standard one---does a within-municipality environmental exposure predict
mortality after fine demographic and spatial-temporal control?---and the
computational shape combines every element of
Sections~\ref{sec:tilted}--\ref{sec:algorithms}.

The data comprise $N = 5{,}000{,}000$ person-year episodes ($35{,}526$
deaths; crude rate $9.5$ per $1{,}000$ person-years) with:
municipality $\times$ year fixed effects ($500 \times 20 = 10{,}000$
groups) absorbed by the profile Gauss--Seidel scheme of Section~\ref{sec:absorb-impl} (Algorithm~S1); a cell-level design of
education (4) $\times$ sex (2) $\times$ age band (12), $J = 96$ cells,
$k = 16$ parameters; and $p = 2$ continuous covariates---standardized log
income and a standardized pollution-exposure index correlated with the
municipality effects (making the fixed effects non-ignorable for it).
Standard errors are clustered on the $500$ municipalities.

The fit: $322$ zero-event municipality--year groups ($159{,}464$
episodes) are dropped by the separation rule of Section~\ref{sec:absorb-impl}; the remaining model---%
$9{,}678$ absorbed effects, $16$ cell-level and $2$ individual-level
coefficients---converges in $8$ tilted Newton iterations,
$11.2$ seconds end to end on one core.  A dense dummy-variable design for
the same model would occupy $N \times 9{,}696$ doubles $\approx 0.4$\,TB
before the first iteration.  For verification at feasible scale, the
subpopulation of the first $10$ municipalities ($n = 97{,}723$, $196$
absorbed groups) was fitted both ways: maximum absolute coefficient
deviation $4.0 \times 10^{-11}$, tilted $0.1$s versus dense $3.0$s.

Substantively, the model recovers the calibrated truth within sampling
error: income $-0.180$ (cluster SE $0.005$; truth $-0.18$), pollution
$+0.049$ (SE $0.005$; truth $+0.06$, within two SEs, identified from
within-municipality variation only, as it must be after absorption),
female $-0.455$ (truth $-0.45$), education gradients $-0.13/-0.27/-0.40$
(truth $-0.12/-0.25/-0.40$), and a monotone age gradient tracking the
Gompertz truth to two decimals.  The point of the exercise is not these
numbers---the generator made them true---but the demonstration that a
model of this shape, with inference, is an interactive-latency
computation under the tilted-moment organization and an infeasible one
under the dense organization; on genuine register data inside a secure
environment, where wall-clock and memory budgets are tight and software
dependencies restricted, this difference is what determines whether such
models get fitted at all.

\section{Discussion}\label{sec:discussion}
% =========================================================================

The paper's message is one computational idea, developed to the point of
routine use: when a GLM mixes a rich categorical structure with a few
continuous covariates, the exact score and information---hence exact
Newton, exact profile Hessians, and exact robust and clustered
covariance matrices---are functions of three tilted within-cell moments
that one streaming pass produces.  Ordinary collapse fails because the
likelihood surface needs the whole within-cell distribution
(Proposition~\ref{prop:sufficiency}); estimation succeeds because each
iteration needs only the current tilt of that distribution
(Theorem~\ref{thm:tilted}).  The experiments quantify what this buys
over the implementations in actual use, including the strongest fair
competitor (a sparse-design IRLS), and the applications show the method
doing routine work on public and register-scale data.

The device composes with, rather than competes against, the
fixed-effect absorption stack: absorbed factors handle dummy structure
too large even for cells, and the tilted pass handles everything else.
The companion theory for that composition---global convergence of the
nonlinear alternating-projections cycle for log-concave links, exact
local rates for any number of absorbed factors from pairwise weighted
cross-tabulations, asymptotics up to the proportional regime
$J/N \to c$ with its Poisson unbiasedness and its logit failure-and-repair,
and Firth and penalized variants computed inside the same pass---is
developed, proved, and validated in the online supplement; we kept it
out of the main text so that the computational contribution stands on
its own terms.

\paragraph{Software.}  The algorithms are implemented twice.  The Stata
command \texttt{stpois} implements the Poisson event-history case in
production form (its software article documents the interface); a
self-contained Python/NumPy reference implementation
(\texttt{tilted\_glm.py}, roughly $800$ lines), which generated every
number in this paper and the supplement, accompanies the manuscript and
is organized to make the correspondence between code and results
line-by-line auditable.  Both are available at
\url{https://github.com/andreasljungstrm/stpois}.

discussions of register-based event-history workflows that motivated
this work.

\section{Proofs for Section~\ref{sec:tilted}}\label{app:tilted}

\subsection{Proof of Proposition~\ref{prop:sufficiency}}

(i) If $\bx_i = \bar\bx_j$ within cells, then
$\eta_i = \bgamma'\bar\bx_j + \bdelta'\bw_j + \log t_i$ and
\eqref{eq:loglik} becomes
$\sum_j \{ (\bgamma'\bar\bx_j + \bdelta'\bw_j) D_j - T_j
e^{\bgamma'\bar\bx_j + \bdelta'\bw_j} \} + \mathrm{const}$, a function of
the displayed cell statistics; the collapsed likelihood is the microdata
likelihood up to an additive constant, so their maximizers and Hessians
coincide.

(ii) For the Poisson family,
\[
\ell(\bgamma, \bdelta)
= \sum_j \Big\{ \bgamma' S_j + \bdelta'\bw_j D_j
   - e^{\bdelta'\bw_j}\, \mathcal{L}_{\nu_j}(\bgamma) \Big\}
   + \mathrm{const},
\]
which establishes computability from $\{S_j, D_j,
\mathcal{L}_{\nu_j}\}$.  Conversely, suppose a statistic permits
evaluation of $\ell$ on an open set $\mathcal{O} \ni (\bgamma_0,
\bdelta_0)$.  The functions $\bdelta \mapsto e^{\bdelta'\bw_j}$ for
distinct vectors $\bw_j$ are linearly independent on any open set, so
from $\bdelta \mapsto \sum_j e^{\bdelta'\bw_j} \mathcal{L}_{\nu_j}
(\bgamma)$ one recovers each $\mathcal{L}_{\nu_j}(\bgamma)$ for each
$\bgamma$ in the open slice.  Each $\nu_j$ is a finite discrete measure,
so $\mathcal{L}_{\nu_j}$ is entire; its values on an open subset of
$\R^p$ determine it everywhere by analytic continuation, and the Laplace
transform of a finite (positive) measure determines the measure.  Hence
the statistic determines $\nu_j$, whose minimal description has dimension
equal to (one plus $p$ times) its support size.

(iii) Jensen's inequality applied to the strictly convex map
$z \mapsto e^z$ under the probability measure $\nu_j / T_j$ gives
$T_j^{-1}\mathcal{L}_{\nu_j}(\bgamma) \ge
\exp(\bgamma' \bar\bx_j)$ with equality iff $\bgamma'\bx$ is
$\nu_j$-a.s.\ constant. \qed

\subsection{Proof of Theorem~\ref{thm:tilted}}

Differentiating \eqref{eq:loglik} with $\theta_i = \eta_i$:
$\phi\,\partial\ell/\partial\bgamma = \sum_i (y_i - b'(\eta_i))\bx_i$ and
$\phi\,\partial\ell/\partial\bdelta = \sum_i (y_i - b'(\eta_i))
\bw_{j(i)}$.  Grouping the second sum by cells and using that $\bw_j$ is
constant within cell $j$ gives $\sum_j \bw_j R_j$; grouping the first
gives $\sum_i y_i \bx_i - \sum_j \mathbf{m}^{b'}_j$ with
$\mathbf{m}^{b'}_j = \sum_{i\in j} b'(\eta_i)\bx_i = B_j
\E_{P^{b'}_{j,\btheta}}[\bx]$ by the definition of the $b'$-tilt.
Differentiating again,
$\phi\,\bF = \sum_i b''(\eta_i)\, \ba_i \ba_i'$ with $\ba_i =
(\bx_i', \bw_{j(i)}')'$; expanding the blocks and grouping by cells
yields (ii), and the moment identities follow from
Definition~\ref{def:tilt}.  For (iii), $b' = b'' = \exp$ gives
$\omega_i = e^{\bdelta'\bw_j}\, t_i e^{\bgamma'\bx_i}$, the cell factor
cancels in the normalization, and $M_j^{(0)} = e^{\bdelta'\bw_j}
\mathcal{L}_{\nu_j}(\bgamma)$; the cumulant identities
$\E_{P_{j,\bgamma}}[\bx] = \nabla K_j$ and $\Var = \nabla^2 K_j$ are the
standard derivatives of a log-Laplace transform, and
$\E[\bx\bx'] = \Var + \E\E'$ gives the stated $\bgamma$-block.  (iv) The
score and information assembled from (i)--(ii) are, entry for entry, the
same matrices as those computed from the stacked design; hence so is the
Newton step. \qed

\subsection{Proof of Corollary~\ref{cor:complexity}}

The microdata pass evaluates, per observation, $\eta_i$ from the cached
cell linear predictor plus $\bgamma'\bx_i$ ($O(p)$), the scalars
$b'(\eta_i), b''(\eta_i)$ ($O(1)$), the binned accumulations of
$\omega_i$ and $\omega_i \bx_i$ into cell $j(i)$ ($O(p)$), and the
symmetric rank-one update $\omega_i \bx_i \bx_i'$ ($O(p^2)$ scalar
multiply--adds, $p(p+1)/2$ distinct); total $O(N(p^2 + p + 1))$.
Assembly of $\sum_j M_j^{(0)}\bw_j\bw_j'$ and $\sum_j \mathbf{m}_j\bw_j'$
costs $O(J(k^2 + kp))$, and the symmetric solve $O((p+k)^3)$.  The dense
iteration forms $\sum_i \omega_i \ba_i\ba_i'$ at $O(N(p+k)^2)$ plus the
same solve.  Ratios follow.  With $p = 0$ the pass reduces to two binned
sums ($R_j$, $M_j^{(0)}$), and if the offsets enter through cell sums
(pure rate models), even these are fixed after one pass. \qed

\subsection{Proof of Proposition~\ref{prop:noncanonical}}

The ML score for a general link is $\sum_i (y_i - \mu_i)
\frac{\partial \mu_i / \partial \eta_i}{V(\mu_i)}\, \ba_i
= \sum_i (y_i - \mu_i)\, g'(\mu_i)\, \omega_i\, \ba_i = \sum_i r_i
\ba_i$, using $\partial\mu/\partial\eta = 1/g'(\mu)$, and the expected
information is $\sum_i \omega_i \ba_i \ba_i'$
\citep[Sec.~2.5]{mccullagh1989}.  These have the same
cell-decomposable form as in Theorem~\ref{thm:tilted} with
$(\omega_i, r_i)$ in place of $(b''(\eta_i), y_i - b'(\eta_i))$, so the
accumulation and complexity claims carry over.  A fixed point of the
scoring iteration has $\sum_i r_i \ba_i = \bzero$, i.e., is a root of
the ML score equations.  For the gamma--log pair, $V(\mu) = \mu^2$ and
$g'(\mu) = 1/\mu$ give $\omega_i = 1/(\mu_i^2 \mu_i^{-2}) = 1$; the
moments \eqref{eq:moments} are then constant in $\btheta$, and only
$R_j = \sum_{i\in j}(y_i - \mu_i)/\mu_i$ requires re-evaluation, an
$O(Np)$ pass. \qed
