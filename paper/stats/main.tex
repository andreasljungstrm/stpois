\begin{abstract}
\noindent
Newton and Fisher-scoring estimation of a generalized linear model
requires, at every iteration, the score vector and the information
matrix of the full $N \times (p + k)$ design.  When $N$ is in the
tens of millions and the design mixes $k$ dummy-coded categorical
columns with $p$ continuous columns, the $O(N(p+k)^2)$ per-iteration
cost and the memory of the design matrix itself become the binding
constraints, and the standard responses---subsampling, sketching,
sparse storage---either abandon exactness or retain an $O(N)$ factor
on the full column dimension.  I establish an exact algebraic
alternative.  For any exponential dispersion family I prove a
\emph{tilted-moment identity}: the score and information at an
arbitrary parameter value are linear combinations of three low-order
moments of an exponentially tilted measure on the cells of the
categorical cross-classification, computable in a single streaming pass
whose cost involves only the continuous columns.  The per-iteration
complexity falls from $O(N(p+k)^2)$ to $O(N(p^2+p+1)) + O(J(k^2+kp))$
for $J$ cells, no design matrix is ever formed, and the resulting
iterates coincide with dense Newton to floating-point reordering
($10^{-13}$--$10^{-15}$ in experiments across Poisson, logistic, and
gamma models): the method is exact, not an approximate-gradient
scheme.  I derive the identity, characterize precisely why no fixed
cell summary can support the likelihood function itself
(a Laplace-transform uniqueness argument covering the Poisson and
Bernoulli families), give the streaming algorithm with its
numerical-stability envelope (log-domain kernels, backtracking,
rank-deficiency and separation handling) and exact cell-accumulated
sandwich and cluster-robust covariance estimators, and benchmark a
reference implementation against dense Newton, sparse-design IRLS, and
production solvers: one to two orders of magnitude faster and
$12$--$50\times$ lighter in memory than \texttt{statsmodels}, faster
than \texttt{glum} and sparse IRLS, with $O(N)$ time and memory
verified to $5 \times 10^7$ observations and accuracy preserved at
information-matrix condition numbers up to $10^{17}$.  As a stress
test, the engine composes with high-dimensional fixed-effect
absorption---profile updates of absorbed factors alternated with
tilted-moment Newton steps---for which I supply a unified global
convergence proof; on public data with $3{,}692$ absorbed fixed
effects the composition reproduces \texttt{pyfixest} to $10^{-9}$ at
lower cost.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}\label{sec:intro}
% =========================================================================

Maximum-likelihood estimation of a generalized linear model
\citep{nelder1972,mccullagh1989} proceeds by Newton--Raphson or Fisher
scoring: at each iteration, form the score vector and the information
matrix of the current iterate and solve a $q$-dimensional linear
system, $q = p + k$ the number of coefficients.  With the design held
as an $N \times q$ matrix, each iteration costs $O(Nq^2)$ floating-point
operations for the weighted Gram product, and the design itself
occupies $Nq$ doubles.  Neither term is problematic at textbook scale.
Both become binding when $N$ reaches $10^7$--$10^8$ and the design has
the composition typical of administrative, clinical, and operational
data: a small number $p$ of continuous covariates alongside a rich
categorical apparatus---strata, batches, regions, periods, and their
interactions---whose dummy coding contributes $k \gg p$ columns.  A
design of $5 \times 10^7$ rows and $500$ dummy columns occupies
$200$\,GB before the first iteration; the Gram product touches all of
it, every iteration.  This is the bottleneck I remove.

The removal is algebraic, not approximate.  Let the categorical
covariates partition the observations into the $J$ cells of their
cross-classification, and let the $k$ dummy-coded columns be constant
within cells while the $p$ continuous columns vary freely.  Two
classical facts frame the problem.  When $p = 0$, the exponential-family
log likelihood collapses onto within-cell aggregates, and estimation on
the $J$ cells reproduces the microdata maximum-likelihood estimator
(MLE) exactly---the standard device of grouped Poisson rate modeling
\citep{frome1983,clayton1993,breslow1987} and, for binary data, of
grouped binomial fitting.  And the collapse is fragile: I show
(Proposition~\ref{prop:sufficiency}) that for $p \ge 1$ \emph{no}
fixed-dimensional cell summary can support the likelihood
\emph{function}, because evaluating the within-cell cumulant sum at all
parameter values determines the Laplace transform of the within-cell
empirical measure of the continuous covariates---and Laplace transforms
determine measures.  The argument is not Poisson folklore: I prove it
for the Poisson and Bernoulli families by one uniqueness argument, so
the impossibility is a property of the exponential family, not of a
particular application.

My starting point is the observation that estimation does not require
the likelihood function; it requires the gradient and
curvature \emph{at the current iterate}, and these admit an exact
finite representation that the function itself does not.
Theorem~\ref{thm:tilted}---the tilted-moment identity---states that for
any exponential dispersion family with canonical link, the score vector
and information matrix at an arbitrary parameter value are linear
combinations of three low-order moments ($M^{(0)}_j$, $\mathbf{m}_j$,
$\bQ$) of an \emph{exponentially tilted} measure on each cell.  For the
Poisson family the tilt is an Esscher transform of the within-cell
exposure measure, free of the cell-level coefficients; for the
Bernoulli--logit family it is the variance-weighted measure that
concentrates on observations near the decision boundary; the identity
is the same in both cases, and Fisher scoring under non-canonical links
has the same structure (Proposition~\ref{prop:noncanonical}), with the
gamma--log family as the limiting case in which the tilt weights are
identically one and the moments are constant across iterations.  The
tilt is the correct \emph{dynamic} object: a fixed collapse fails
because it attempts likelihood exactness, which
Proposition~\ref{prop:sufficiency} forbids; the tilted collapse
succeeds because it needs only score exactness, iterate by iterate.

The identity converts directly into complexity, which I record as the
scaling proposition (Corollary~\ref{cor:complexity}): one Newton or
Fisher-scoring iteration costs a single streaming
$O(N(p^2 + p + 1))$ pass over the microdata---the continuous columns
only---plus $O(J(k^2 + kp))$ cell-level assembly and one
$O((p+k)^3)$ solve, against $O(N(p+k)^2)$ for the full design.  The
saving grows quadratically in the categorical richness $k$; the memory
footprint contains no $Nk$ term at all, since no design matrix is ever
formed; and, because the identity is exact rather than a linearization,
the iterates coincide with dense Newton up to floating-point
reordering, which Section~\ref{sec:sim-validation} verifies at
$10^{-13}$--$10^{-15}$, iterate by iterate, for Poisson, logistic, and
gamma models.  The distinction matters for how the method should be
classified: it is not a stochastic-gradient, subsampled, or sketched
solver with an accuracy--cost dial, but an exact reorganization of
Newton's arithmetic whose output \emph{is} the MLE with its full
inferential apparatus---observed information, sandwich, and
cluster-robust covariance matrices, all accumulated cellwise in the
same pass \citep{huber1967,white1980,liang1986}.

In the second half I develop the identity into a solver and measure
it.  Section~\ref{sec:algorithms} gives the streaming
algorithm and its numerical envelope: log-domain likelihood kernels,
backtracking line search, rank-deficiency and separation handling
\citep{wedderburn1976,albert1984}, exact cell-accumulated covariance
estimators, memory layout, and parallel structure.
Section~\ref{sec:simulations} contains the experiments, organized
around three comparisons: dense Newton as the ground-truth baseline for
exactness; sparse-design IRLS---the strongest general-purpose
competitor, since dummy columns are the textbook sparse design---for
the like-for-like cost comparison; and the production solvers
\texttt{statsmodels} \citep{seabold2010} and \texttt{glum}
\citep{tabmat2021} for real-world viability, together with $O(N)$
scaling to $5\times10^7$ observations and a numerical-stability battery
spanning condition numbers to $10^{17}$.  Section~\ref{sec:applications}
exercises the solver on public data (a logistic on-time-performance
model for the $336{,}776$ flights of \texttt{nycflights13}, verified
against \texttt{statsmodels} to $10^{-13}$) and on a
$5\times10^6$-observation Poisson benchmark.

One structured application receives separate treatment because it is
the natural stress test of the engine: high-dimensional fixed-effect
(HDFE) absorption, where one or more factors---firms, aircraft,
region--period interactions \citep{abowd1999}---have too many levels
even for cell-level treatment and are concentrated out by profile
updates alternated with tilted-moment Newton steps
(Section~\ref{sec:absorb-impl}).  The composition inherits the
engine's economics: the absorbed factors never enter any design either.
Convergence of the linear inner demeaning loop is classical---the
method of alternating projections of von Neumann and
\citet{halperin1962}, brought into fixed-effects estimation by
\citet{gaure2013}---and the combined nonlinear GLM cycle has long been
validated by simulation \citep{guimaraes2010,berge2018,correia2020};
what has been missing is a single formal theorem for the combined
inner--outer iteration, which I state as Theorem~\ref{thm:global-main}
(proof in the supplement) and verify against \texttt{pyfixest} on real
data with $3{,}692$ absorbed effects to $10^{-9}$.  The supplement
develops the associated rate and asymptotic theory; in the main text
the absorption path is strictly an application of the tilting engine,
not a second subject.

\paragraph{Relation to existing computational methods.}
The method occupies a specific point in the landscape of large-$N$ GLM
computation, and its novelty is best stated by contrast.
\emph{Approximate data-reduction methods}---optimal subsampling for
logistic regression \citep{wang2018}, information-based subdata
selection \citep{wang2019}, leverage-score sampling \citep{ma2015},
local case-control sampling for rare events \citep{fithian2014}, and
randomized sketching \citep{drineas2016}---trade statistical accuracy
for cost along a tunable frontier; the tilted pass is exact and
deterministic, so where a cell structure exists it dominates this
frontier pointwise rather than moving along it.  \emph{Collapsed
sufficient statistics} for grouped rate and binomial models
\citep{frome1983,clayton1993} are the special case $p = 0$; the tilted
moments extend the exact collapse to $p \ge 1$, which is precisely the
case Proposition~\ref{prop:sufficiency} shows a fixed collapse cannot
reach.  \emph{Matrix-free and Hessian-free Newton methods}
\citep{pearlmutter1994,martens2010,nocedal2006} avoid storing the
Hessian by supplying Hessian--vector products to an iterative solver;
they still stream the full design through every product and solve the
$q$-dimensional system approximately, whereas the tilted representation
forms the \emph{exact} $(p+k)$-dimensional system in closed form from
cell moments and solves it directly.  \emph{Grouped or stochastic
second-order methods} \citep{byrd2011} subsample curvature; the tilted
step is exact.  \emph{Automatic differentiation} \citep{baydin2018}
mechanizes the same derivatives at $O(N(p+k)^2)$; the contribution here
is the analytic identity that makes the derivative a cell-level object.
\emph{Sparse-design and bounded-memory solvers}
(\texttt{glum}, \citealp{tabmat2021}; \texttt{biglm},
\citealp{lumley2011}) store the dummy block sparsely or stream the
design in chunks; Section~\ref{sec:sim-baselines} shows the sparse route
is the strongest general competitor, and that the tilted pass wins
because it never materializes a design at all and its cost grows only
in the continuous dimension.  \emph{Distributed and
communication-avoiding optimization} \citep{zhang2013,jaggi2014}
splits one fit across machines, an orthogonal axis: the tilted
accumulators are additive across data partitions, so the pass
distributes trivially per shard.  Finally, the \emph{HDFE stack}
\citep{guimaraes2010,gaure2013,correia2016,correia2020,berge2018,
stammann2018} removes dummy factors by iterative demeaning but streams
the full non-absorbed design through every IRLS step; the two devices
compose (Section~\ref{sec:absorb-impl}), with the tilted pass handling
the cell-structured block that HDFE tools treat as ordinary columns.
In summary: the contribution is neither ``a sufficient statistic
exists'' nor ``regroup the sums,'' but the exact, iterate-dependent
tilted-moment identity (Theorem~\ref{thm:tilted}) that turns the score
and the full information matrix of a mixed categorical--continuous GLM
into a one-pass cell computation, together with its complexity, memory,
and stability consequences.

All numerical results regenerate from a self-contained open-source
Python/NumPy reference implementation released with the manuscript; a
companion Stata command (\texttt{stpois}), distributed in the same
repository, implements the Poisson event-history case for applied use.

\section{Exponential family models and the failure of ordinary
collapse}\label{sec:tilted}
% =========================================================================

\subsection{Model and notation}\label{sec:model}

Observations $i = 1, \dots, N$ carry a response $y_i$, a vector
$\bx_i \in \R^{p}$ of \emph{individual-level} covariates (those involving
at least one continuous variable, including continuous--categorical
interaction columns), a vector $\bw_{j(i)} \in \R^{k}$ of
\emph{cell-level} covariates, and a known offset $o_i$.  The map
$j : \{1,\dots,N\} \to \{1,\dots,J\}$ assigns each observation to the
cell of the cross-classification of the categorical covariates;
$\bw_j$ is constant within cell $j$ by construction.  Conditional on the
covariates, responses are independent draws from an exponential
dispersion family
\begin{equation}\label{eq:edf}
f(y_i \mid \theta_i, \phi)
  = \exp\!\left\{ \frac{y_i \theta_i - b(\theta_i)}{\phi}
      + c(y_i, \phi) \right\},
\end{equation}
with $b : \Theta \to \R$ strictly convex and three times continuously
differentiable on the open canonical parameter space $\Theta \subseteq \R$,
and dispersion $\phi > 0$
\citep{nelder1972,jorgensen1987,mccullagh1989}.  The mean and variance
are $\mu_i = b'(\theta_i)$ and $\Var(y_i) = \phi\, b''(\theta_i)$.  I
model the linear predictor
\begin{equation}\label{eq:linpred}
\eta_i = \bgamma' \bx_i + \bdelta' \bw_{j(i)} + o_i,
\qquad \btheta = (\bgamma', \bdelta')' \in \R^{q}, \quad q = p + k,
\end{equation}
and, until Section~\ref{sec:noncanonical}, take the link to be canonical,
$\theta_i = \eta_i$.  Two members of the family serve as running
examples throughout, on equal footing.  \emph{Poisson}:
$b(\theta) = e^{\theta}$, $\phi = 1$, $y_i$ a count, and
$o_i = \log t_i$ a log exposure; with episode-split survival data this
is the piecewise-exponential event-history likelihood
\citep{holford1980,aitkin1980,andersen1993}.  \emph{Bernoulli--logit}:
$b(\theta) = \log(1 + e^{\theta})$, $\phi = 1$, $y_i \in \{0, 1\}$---the
workhorse for binary outcomes at scale.  The gamma family with log link
enters in Section~\ref{sec:noncanonical} as the canonical illustration
of the non-canonical case.  Up to terms not involving $\btheta$, the
log likelihood is
\begin{equation}\label{eq:loglik}
\ell(\btheta)
  = \frac{1}{\phi} \sum_{i=1}^{N}
      \left\{ y_i \eta_i - b(\eta_i) \right\}.
\end{equation}
Because \eqref{eq:loglik} is a sum of concave functions of the linear
predictor, $\ell$ is concave on $\R^q$, strictly so on the quotient of
$\R^q$ by the null space of the stacked design
$\ba_i = (\bx_i', \bw_{j(i)}')'$.  Write
$\bU(\btheta) = \nabla \ell$ for the score,
$\bH(\btheta) = -\nabla^2 \ell$ for the negative Hessian, and note that
for canonical links $\bH$ equals the Fisher information
$\bF(\btheta) = \E[-\nabla^2 \ell]$, so Newton--Raphson and Fisher scoring
coincide.

\subsection{Cell collapse and its failure}\label{sec:sufficiency}

Suppose first that $p = 0$: every covariate is categorical, so
$\eta_i = \bdelta'\bw_j + o_i$ varies over $i$ within a cell only through
the offset.  For the Poisson family with $o_i = \log t_i$,
\eqref{eq:loglik} becomes
$\ell(\bdelta) = \sum_j \{ D_j\, \bdelta'\bw_j - T_j e^{\bdelta'\bw_j} \}
+ \mathrm{const}$, with $D_j = \sum_{i \in j} y_i$ and
$T_j = \sum_{i \in j} t_i$: the collapsed cell data $(D_j, T_j)$ carry
all the information.  For the Bernoulli--logit family with constant
offsets the same collapse holds with $(D_j, n_j)$, the cell event count
and cell size---grouped binomial fitting.  In both cases estimation on
the $J$ cells is exact, at $O(Jq^2)$ per iteration.  The following
proposition records why no analogous finite collapse survives the
introduction of a continuous covariate, and in what precise sense; the
argument is a property of the family's cumulant function, and I give it
in one form that covers both running examples.  Define the within-cell
\emph{weighted covariate measure}
$\nu_j = \sum_{i \in j} e^{o_i} \delta_{\bx_i}$ on $\R^p$ (for Poisson,
the exposure measure $\nu_j = \sum_{i \in j} t_i \delta_{\bx_i}$; for
Bernoulli without offsets, the empirical measure of the continuous
covariates), a finite discrete measure recording the joint configuration
of offsets and continuous covariates in cell $j$, and write
$\mathcal{L}_{\nu_j}(\bgamma) = \int e^{\bgamma'\bx}\,\nu_j(\dd\bx)$ for
its Laplace transform.

\begin{proposition}[Failure of fixed collapse]\label{prop:sufficiency}
Let the cumulant $b$ be real-analytic on $\R$ with
$b(u) = e^{u}\,(1 + o(1))$ as $u \to -\infty$---as holds for the Poisson
family, where $b(u) = e^u$ exactly, and the Bernoulli family, where
$b(u) = \log(1 + e^u)$.
(i) If $(\bx_i, o_i) = (\bar\bx_j, \bar o_j)$ for all $i \in j$ and all
$j$, the log likelihood \eqref{eq:loglik} depends on the data only
through $\{(D_j, n_j, \bar\bx_j, \bar o_j, \sum_{i\in j} y_i
\bx_i)\}_{j \le J}$, and the MLE on the collapsed cells equals the
microdata MLE; for Poisson the offset need not be constant, and
$(D_j, T_j)$ suffices as above.
(ii) In general, $\ell(\bgamma, \bdelta)$ depends on the data through
$\{ S_j, D_j, G_j \}_{j \le J}$, where
$S_j = \sum_{i \in j} y_i \bx_i$ and
$G_j(s, \bgamma) = \sum_{i \in j} b(s + \bgamma'\bx_i + o_i)$ is the
within-cell cumulant functional; and any statistic from which the
likelihood function can be evaluated for $(\bgamma, \bdelta)$ in an open
set determines $G_j$ on an open set, hence---by real-analyticity in $s$
and the boundary expansion
$e^{-s} G_j(s, \bgamma) \to \mathcal{L}_{\nu_j}(\bgamma)$ as
$s \to -\infty$---determines the Laplace transform of $\nu_j$, and
therefore $\nu_j$ itself.  The support size of $\nu_j$, and with it the
dimension of any such statistic, grows with the number of distinct
covariate values in the cell: no fixed-dimensional cell summary can
support the likelihood function.
(iii) By strict convexity of the exponential (Jensen),
$\mathcal{L}_{\nu_j}(\bgamma) \ge \nu_j(\R^p)\,
e^{\bgamma' \bar\bx_j}$ with equality along a direction of $\bgamma$
only if $\bgamma'\bx$ is $\nu_j$-a.s.\ constant along it: replacing
microdata by cell means strictly understates the within-cell mass of
the cumulant term and is not a reparametrization of the same
likelihood---the same convexity mechanism that biases log-linearized
estimation relative to Poisson pseudo-maximum-likelihood
\citep{santossilva2006}.
\end{proposition}

Part (ii) is the formal statement of the practical dictum that
``continuous covariates break collapsibility,'' and it holds uniformly
across the family: the fixed-dimensional sufficient statistic
$\sum_i y_i \ba_i$ for the canonical parameter still exists, but
\emph{computing the likelihood function}---and hence running any
optimizer that evaluates it---requires functionals of the data whose
dimension is of order $N$, not $J$.  The next section shows that the
required functionals nevertheless have a special structure at any
\emph{single} parameter value: they are three low-order moments of a
tilted measure, computable in one linear pass.  Estimation needs only
this pointwise object.

\section{Tilted cell moments}\label{sec:tilted-main}
% =========================================================================

\subsection{Exponentially tilted cell moments}\label{sec:tilted-moments}

\begin{definition}[Tilted within-cell measures]\label{def:tilt}
Fix $\btheta = (\bgamma, \bdelta)$ and let $\omega_i =
b''(\eta_i) > 0$ be the information weight of observation $i$.  The
\emph{information-tilted measure} on cell $j$ is the probability measure
\[
P_{j,\btheta}(\{i\}) = \frac{\omega_i}{\sum_{i' \in j} \omega_{i'}},
\qquad i \in j .
\]
For the Poisson family, $\omega_i = \mu_i = t_i
e^{\bdelta'\bw_j + \bgamma'\bx_i}$, the factor $e^{\bdelta'\bw_j}$
cancels, and $P_{j,\btheta}$ reduces to the \emph{Esscher transform}
(exponential tilt) of the normalized exposure measure $\nu_j / T_j$:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:esscher}
P_{j,\bgamma}(\dd\bx)
 = \frac{e^{\bgamma'\bx}\,\nu_j(\dd\bx)}{\mathcal{L}_{\nu_j}(\bgamma)},
\end{equation}
free of $\bdelta$.  Its moments are derivatives of the within-cell
cumulant generating function: with
$K_j(\bgamma) = \log \mathcal{L}_{\nu_j}(\bgamma)$,
$\E_{P_{j,\bgamma}}[\bx] = \nabla K_j(\bgamma)$ and
$\Var_{P_{j,\bgamma}}(\bx) = \nabla^2 K_j(\bgamma)$.
\end{definition}

The tilt \eqref{eq:esscher} is precisely the change of measure that
underlies exponential-family conjugation and saddlepoint approximation
\citep{barndorff1978,daniels1954,jensen1995} and exponential-tilting
estimators in the empirical-likelihood family
\citep{efron1981,kitamura1997,owen2001,schennach2007}; here it appears not as an
approximation device but as an exact algebraic identity for the score and
information, as follows.  Write, for each cell $j$,
\begin{equation}\label{eq:moments}
M_j^{(0)} = \sum_{i \in j} \omega_i, \qquad
\mathbf{m}_j = \sum_{i \in j} \omega_i \bx_i, \qquad
\bQ = \sum_{i=1}^N \omega_i \bx_i \bx_i' ,
\end{equation}
and let $B_j = \sum_{i \in j} b'(\eta_i)$ and
$R_j = \sum_{i \in j} (y_i - b'(\eta_i))$ denote the cell sums of means
and of raw residuals.

\begin{theorem}[Exact tilted-moment representation]\label{thm:tilted}
For the model \eqref{eq:edf}--\eqref{eq:linpred} with canonical link, at
every $\btheta \in \R^q$:
\begin{enumerate}
\item[(i)] The score is
\[
\phi\, \bU(\btheta) =
\begin{pmatrix}
\sum_{i} y_i \bx_i - \sum_{j} \mathbf{m}^{b'}_j \\[2pt]
\sum_{j} \bw_j R_j
\end{pmatrix},
\qquad
\mathbf{m}^{b'}_j = \sum_{i \in j} b'(\eta_i)\,\bx_i
  = B_j\, \E_{P^{b'}_{j,\btheta}}[\bx],
\]
where $P^{b'}_{j,\btheta}$ is the tilt of Definition~\ref{def:tilt} with
$b'$ in place of $b''$.
\item[(ii)] The information matrix is
\[
\phi\, \bF(\btheta) =
\begin{pmatrix}
\bQ & \sum_j \mathbf{m}_j \bw_j' \\[2pt]
\sum_j \bw_j \mathbf{m}_j' & \sum_j M_j^{(0)} \bw_j \bw_j'
\end{pmatrix},
\qquad
\mathbf{m}_j = M_j^{(0)}\, \E_{P_{j,\btheta}}[\bx],
\quad
\bQ = \sum_j M_j^{(0)}\, \E_{P_{j,\btheta}}[\bx\bx'] .
\]
\item[(iii)] For the Poisson family, $b' = b''$, the two tilts coincide
with the $\bdelta$-free Esscher transform \eqref{eq:esscher}, and
$B_j = M_j^{(0)} = e^{\bdelta'\bw_j} \mathcal{L}_{\nu_j}(\bgamma)$,
$\mathbf{m}_j = M_j^{(0)} \nabla K_j(\bgamma)$,
and the $\bgamma$-block of $\bF$ is
$\sum_j M_j^{(0)} \{ \nabla^2 K_j(\bgamma) + \nabla K_j \nabla K_j' \}$.
For the Bernoulli--logit family, $b''(\eta_i) = \mu_i(1 - \mu_i)$: the
information tilt weights each within-cell observation by its Bernoulli
variance, concentrating $P_{j,\btheta}$ on observations near the
decision boundary; the tilt depends on $\bdelta$ through $\mu_i$, so the
moments \eqref{eq:moments} are re-accumulated at each iterate---which is
exactly what the single pass does.
\item[(iv)] The Newton step
$\btheta^{+} = \btheta + \bF(\btheta)^{-1} \bU(\btheta)$ computed from the
quantities in (i)--(ii) is identical to the Newton step computed from the
full $N \times q$ design.
\end{enumerate}
\end{theorem}

Claim (iv) is immediate once (i)--(ii) are established---the two
computations produce the same matrices---but it is the operative point:
the decomposition involves no linearization, discretization, or
stochastic approximation, so the accelerated algorithm is not a fast
approximate estimator but a fast exact organization of the arithmetic of
the MLE.  Section~\ref{sec:sim-validation} exhibits agreement of the full
iterate \emph{sequences} at the $10^{-13}$ level, which is the accumulation
of floating-point reordering only.

Statistically, the representation has content beyond bookkeeping.  The
$\bdelta$-score in (i) compares each cell's observed event mass with its
expected mass under the current tilt; the cross-information in (ii) is
the tilted covariance between cell-level and individual-level design; and
the $\bgamma$-block in (iii) decomposes into a within-cell tilted
covariance $\nabla^2 K_j$ plus a between-cell term.  In particular the
identification of $\bgamma$ rests exactly on
$\sum_j M_j^{(0)} \nabla^2 K_j(\bgamma) \succ \bzero$: continuous
covariates are identified through their \emph{within-cell, tilt-weighted}
variation---a fact that the block structure displays, and that drives
the Schur-complement profile Hessians used throughout the companion
theory in the online supplement.  (When a continuous covariate is
constant within cells---a route-level distance, say---it remains
identified through between-cell variation as long as the cell-level
design $\bw_j$ is not saturated; Section~\ref{sec:app-flights}
exercises this case.)

\begin{corollary}[Scaling]\label{cor:complexity}
One Newton iteration computed via Theorem~\ref{thm:tilted} costs
\[
\underbrace{O\!\left(N(p^2 + p + 1)\right)}_{\text{microdata pass}}
+ \underbrace{O\!\left(J(k^2 + kp)\right)}_{\text{cell assembly}}
+ \underbrace{O\!\left((p+k)^3\right)}_{\text{solve}}
\]
floating-point operations and one $O(N)$ memory sweep, versus
$O\!\left(N(p+k)^2\right) + O\!\left((p+k)^3\right)$ for the full-design
iteration.  When $N \gg J(p+k)$ the leading-order saving is the factor
$(p+k)^2 / (p^2+p+1)$, which grows quadratically in the richness $k$ of
the categorical structure; when additionally $p = 0$ the microdata pass
degenerates to the classical cell collapse and the entire iteration runs
at $O(Jk^2)$.
\end{corollary}

Two remarks on the constant factors.  First, the microdata pass is a
fused stream: one evaluation of $b'(\eta_i)$, $b''(\eta_i)$ per
observation, $p+1$ multiply--accumulate reductions into cell bins, and a
rank-one update of the $p \times p$ Gram matrix; on modern hardware it is
memory-bandwidth-bound, so measured speedups (Section~\ref{sec:sim-validation})
are large but smaller than the FLOP ratio.  Second, the accumulations are
plain segmented sums---\texttt{bincount} operations---so they
parallelize and stream trivially; no $N \times q$ design matrix is ever
formed, which brings a problem whose dense design exceeds working memory
(Section~\ref{sec:app-register}) within a laptop's footprint.

\subsection{Non-canonical links}\label{sec:noncanonical}

So far the link has been canonical, $\theta_i = \eta_i$, where the
Newton weight $b''(\eta_i)$ and the tilt coincide.  The construction does
not depend on this.  For a general link $g$ with $\mu_i = g^{-1}(\eta_i)$
the maximum-likelihood score for a linear exponential family is
$\sum_i (y_i - \mu_i)\,\frac{\partial\mu_i/\partial\eta_i}{V(\mu_i)}\,
\ba_i$ and the expected (Fisher) information is
$\sum_i \omega_i \ba_i\ba_i'$, with the standard IRLS weight and
working residual
\begin{equation}\label{eq:fisher-weight}
\omega_i = \frac{1}{V(\mu_i)\, g'(\mu_i)^2}, \qquad
r_i = (y_i - \mu_i)\, g'(\mu_i)\,\omega_i
\end{equation}
\citep[Sec.~2.5]{mccullagh1989}---the quasi-likelihood weighting of
\citet{wedderburn1974}, whose IRLS/Gauss--Newton correspondence is
developed by \citet{green1984}---where $V$ is the variance function and
$g'(\mu) = \partial\eta/\partial\mu$.  Comparing with
Theorem~\ref{thm:tilted}, the only change is that the pair
$(b'(\eta_i), b''(\eta_i)) = (\mu_i, \omega_i^{\mathrm{can}})$ of the
canonical case is replaced by $(\mu_i, \omega_i)$ and the raw residual
$y_i - \mu_i$ by $r_i$: the tilt is now weighted by the Fisher weight
\eqref{eq:fisher-weight}, and everything downstream---the cell sums, the
Gram matrix, the block assembly---is unchanged.

\begin{proposition}[Tilted Fisher scoring]\label{prop:noncanonical}
With $\omega_i$ and $r_i$ as in \eqref{eq:fisher-weight}, the
expected-information scoring step admits the representation of
Theorem~\ref{thm:tilted}, with $M_j^{(0)}, \mathbf{m}_j, \bQ$ built from
$\omega_i$ and $R_j = \sum_{i \in j} r_i$; the fixed points of the
iteration are exactly the roots of the ML score equations, and
Corollary~\ref{cor:complexity} applies verbatim.
\end{proposition}

Two points of intuition and scope.  First, whether the moments must be
\emph{re-accumulated} each iteration depends on how $\omega_i$ depends on
$\eta_i$.  For canonical links $\omega_i = b''(\eta_i)$ moves with the
iterate, so a fresh pass is needed---which is the pass the algorithm
takes anyway.  The gamma family with log link is the striking exception:
$V(\mu) = \mu^2$ and $g'(\mu) = 1/\mu$ give $\omega_i \equiv 1$, so the
Fisher weight is constant, the ``tilted'' moments are ordinary
exposure-weighted cell moments \emph{fixed across iterations}, and after
one pass each subsequent iteration costs $O(Np) + O(Jk^2)$---the
acceleration is at its most extreme precisely where the link is
non-canonical.  Second, the representation needs only that
$\omega_i > 0$ and $V, g$ be smooth on the range of the fitted means, so
it covers the standard non-canonical GLMs---gamma or inverse-Gaussian
with log link, binomial with probit or complementary log-log, Poisson
with identity or square-root link---as expected-information scoring.  It
does not by itself guarantee that scoring \emph{converges globally} for
these links; that requires the log-concavity condition made precise for
the absorbed-factor problem in Supplement Theorem~S1, which holds for
gamma--log and probit but not for every link.  Observed information
(true Newton) under a non-canonical link adds a term in
$b'''$/\,$g''$ that is equally cell-decomposable but unnecessary here,
since Fisher scoring already delivers the MLE at the same per-iteration
cost.


\subsection{Families in scope}\label{sec:families-main}

Three instances are carried through the experiments, chosen to span
the structural cases rather than to catalogue applications.
\emph{Poisson with log link} (canonical; $\bdelta$-free Esscher tilt
\eqref{eq:esscher}, moments computable from within-cell cumulant
generating functions) and \emph{Bernoulli with logit link} (canonical;
$\bdelta$-dependent variance tilt, moments re-accumulated per iterate)
are the two running examples and receive equal treatment throughout.
\emph{Gamma with log link} (non-canonical,
Proposition~\ref{prop:noncanonical}) is the boundary case of the Fisher
weight: $\omega_i \equiv 1$, so the ``tilted'' moments are ordinary
cell moments, fixed across iterations, and after the first pass each
iteration costs $O(Np) + O(Jk^2)$.  Together the three exhibit the full
range of tilt behavior---exactly exponential, iterate-dependent, and
constant.

\subsection{The streaming pass}\label{sec:streaming}

Algorithm~\ref{alg:tilted} states the method operationally.  Its
preprocessing is one factorization of the categorical covariates into
cell identifiers $j(i)$ and one construction of the $J \times k$
cell-level design $\bW$; both are $O(N)$ given a sort or hash of the
categorical columns, and both are shared across models on the same
cells.

\begin{algorithm}[t]
\caption{Tilted-moment Newton / Fisher scoring}\label{alg:tilted}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\State Precompute cell ids $j(i)$, cell design $\bW \in \R^{J \times k}$,
  continuous columns $\bX \in \R^{N \times p}$, offsets $o_i$;
  initialize $\btheta_0 = (\bgamma_0, \bdelta_0)$.
\Repeat
  \State \emph{Stream over $i = 1, \dots, N$:} compute
    $\eta_i = \bgamma'\bx_i + (\bW\bdelta)_{j(i)} + o_i$, the weight
    $\omega_i$, and the residual $r_i$; accumulate
    $M^{(0)}_{j(i)} \mathrel{+}= \omega_i$;\;
    $\mathbf{m}_{j(i)} \mathrel{+}= \omega_i \bx_i$;\;
    $R_{j(i)} \mathrel{+}= r_i$;\;
    $\bU_\gamma \mathrel{+}= r_i \bx_i$;\;
    $\bQ \mathrel{+}= \omega_i \bx_i \bx_i'$.
  \State \emph{Cell assembly:} $\bU_\delta = \bW'\mathbf{R}$;\;
    $\bF_{\gamma\delta} = \mathbf{m}'\bW$;\;
    $\bF_{\delta\delta} = \bW'\diag(M^{(0)})\bW$;\;
    $\bF_{\gamma\gamma} = \bQ$.
  \State Solve the $(p{+}k)$-dimensional system
    $\bF \Delta = \bU$; update
    $\btheta \gets \btheta + \lambda \Delta$ with backtracking
    $\lambda \in \{1, \tfrac12, \dots\}$ on the log likelihood.
\Until{$\max_\ell |\Delta\theta_\ell|/(1 + |\theta_\ell|) < \tau$}
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

By Theorem~\ref{thm:tilted}(iv) the iterate sequence is identical to
full-design Newton from the same start; every design decision below
concerns constants, not correctness.

\subsection{Numerical safeguards}\label{sec:stability}

Three matter in practice.  (i) \emph{Monotonicity}: the backtracking
line search on the log likelihood guarantees ascent and terminates,
since the Newton direction of a positive-definite system is an ascent
direction; in well-conditioned problems the full step is accepted after
the first iterations and convergence is quadratic.  (ii) \emph{Log-domain
evaluation}: likelihood and mean evaluations use \texttt{log1p} /
\texttt{logaddexp}-style kernels so that extreme linear predictors
(produced, e.g., en route to a separated fit) yield $-\infty$ log
likelihoods rather than overflow.  (iii) \emph{Rank deficiency and
separation}: existence and uniqueness of the GLM MLE can fail
\citep{wedderburn1976}, classically under complete or quasi-complete
separation in the binary case \citep{albert1984} and under analogous
boundary configurations for Poisson
\citep{santossilva2010,santossilva2011,correia2019}.  If the assembled
$\bF$ is singular---collinear cell-level columns, or vanishing weights
under quasi-separation---the solve falls back to a minimum-norm
(pseudoinverse) direction and the iteration runs to its cap with the
non-convergence flagged, never silently; separation is a property of the
data, not of the algorithm.  Section~S3 of the supplement develops the
bias-reducing adjusted-score variant of \citet{firth1993}---the standard
finite-maximizer remedy \citep{heinze2002,kosmidis2009}---computed
inside the same streaming pass at the same per-iteration order.

\subsection{Memory layout and parallelism}\label{sec:memory}

The gap between measured and FLOP ratios merits a constructive comment,
since closing it is an implementation exercise with a known playbook.
The tilted pass streams $O(Np)$ numbers and performs $O(Np^2)$ flops, so
for small $p$ its arithmetic intensity is low and the memory bus, not
the ALU, sets the speed; the dense baseline, by contrast, is a BLAS-3
Gram update running near peak.  Three standard remedies raise the tilted
pass toward bandwidth saturation and beyond: (i) \emph{cache locality}:
storing the microdata sorted by cell converts the binned accumulations
into contiguous segmented sums, so each cell's partial moments stay in
registers/L1 and the pass touches each cache line once, the streaming
analogue of the classical one-pass moment updates of \citet{welford1962}
(cf.\ \citealp{correia2016}); (ii) \emph{fusion}: the weight evaluation,
residual, binned sums, and Gram update share one loop, so the data are
read once per iteration rather than once per moment; and (iii)
\emph{parallelism}: cells are independent, so the pass shards trivially
across threads with per-thread partial moments and a $J k$-sized
reduction, with no atomics on the hot path---the same structure GPU
segmented-reduction primitives expect.  The single-threaded NumPy
reference implementation uses none of (i)--(iii) beyond what
\texttt{bincount} provides, so the reported speedups should be read as a
floor; the FLOP ratio governs how the comparison scales with $(p, k)$,
and the bandwidth analysis governs the attainable constant.

The \emph{memory} footprint is where the reorganization is most
decisive, and it is easy to state exactly.  The tilted pass holds the
loaded columns, $O(N(p+1))$ doubles, plus the accumulators: $J$ cell
scalars, a $J \times p$ moment array, and the $(p+k) \times (p+k)$
information---$O(N(p+1)) + O(J(k+p) + (p+k)^2)$ in total, with no term in
$Nk$.  A dense design is $N(p+k)$ doubles, and a general-purpose IRLS
adds working copies on top; the measured peak resident sets bear this out
(Tables~\ref{tab:sim1b} and~\ref{tab:scaling}): $\sim\!80$\,MB for the
tilted fit against $\sim\!1$\,GB dense and $\sim\!4$\,GB for
\texttt{statsmodels} at $N = 2\times10^5$, widening to $3.8$\,GB versus a
$200$\,GB dense design at $N = 5\times10^7$.  Because peak memory, not
FLOPs, is what makes a register-scale fit feasible or not, this is the
practically load-bearing comparison.  Preprocessing---the one-time sort
or hash that builds the cell identifiers and, for absorbed factors, the
group indices---is $O(N \log N)$ or $O(N)$ and is shared across all
models fitted on the same cells (a sweep of specifications, a
bootstrap, a cross-validation), so it is reported separately from the
per-iteration cost throughout and is amortized whenever more than one
model is fitted on a design.

\paragraph{Reproducibility of the timings.}  All wall-clock and
peak-memory figures are measured on a single core of a
commodity x86-64 workstation (AVX2, $32$\,KiB L1 / $1$\,MiB L2 per core,
shared L3), with each competitor fitted in a fresh process so that peak
resident set isolates its footprint; times are medians over repeated
fits.  The reference implementation is pure NumPy/SciPy on the system
BLAS, single-threaded (BLAS threads pinned to one) so that the comparison
reflects algorithmic organization rather than parallel speedup, which
Section~\ref{sec:memory} argues the tilted pass admits but does not yet
use.  The dense-side factorizations and the pseudoinverse fallback follow
standard practice \citep{golub2013}.  The exact hardware affects the
constants, not the ratios or the $O(\cdot)$ trends, which are the
claims I make; every figure regenerates from the scripts in the code
supplement.

\subsection{Robust and clustered variance estimation}\label{sec:vce}

All variance estimators used in practice share the cell structure.  The
observed/expected information is $\bF$ itself (Theorem~\ref{thm:tilted}).
The Huber--White sandwich estimator
\citep{huber1967,white1980,white1982}---the default under conditional-mean
correctness without full distributional trust
\citep{gourieroux1984}---needs $\sum_i \hat u_i^2 \ba_i \ba_i'$ with
$\hat u_i = y_i - \hat\mu_i$; its blocks accumulate cellwise exactly like
\eqref{eq:moments} with $\omega_i$ replaced by $\hat u_i^2$.  The
cluster-robust estimator \citep{liang1986,cameron2011} sums scores within
clusters before the outer product; when clusters are unions of cells (the
common design: cluster on region, cells within region) the accumulation
is again one pass.  These identities are what make \emph{inference}, not
just point estimation, run at cell-collapsed cost; they are implemented
in the reference code and validated against dense computation in the
test suite.



\subsection{A stress test: absorbed fixed-effect factors}
\label{sec:absorb-impl}

The natural stress test of the tilting engine is the setting where some
factors have too many levels even for cell-level treatment---the worker
and firm effects of \citet{abowd1999}, hospitals, aircraft,
municipality--year interactions---and are removed by \emph{absorption}
rather than estimation.  The engine composes with the standard
absorption device: augment the linear predictor with $G$ absorbed
factors $\alpha^{(g)}_{g(i)}$, concentrate them out by cycling
closed-form profile updates (for Poisson, $\alpha^{(g)}_a =
\log\{\sum_{i: g(i)=a} y_i / \sum_{i: g(i)=a} \exp(\eta_i -
\alpha^{(g)}_a)\}$, the multiplicative update of
\citealp{guimaraes2010,berge2018,correia2020}, used at scale in
three-way gravity estimation by \citealp{larch2019}), and take each
tilted-moment Newton step on $(\bgamma, \bdelta)$ at the profiled
effects, with the inner cycle re-solved at every trial point of the line
search (Algorithm~S1 of the supplement).  Each inner sweep is $O(N)$;
with a single absorbed factor the inner solve is exact in one sweep.
The composition inherits the engine's economics: absorbed factors, like
cells, never enter any design matrix.

This inner--outer scheme is the algorithm used across the HDFE--GLM
software ecosystem, and its convergence status deserves a precise
statement.  Convergence of the \emph{inner} loop---iterative demeaning
over the absorbed factors at fixed weights---is classical: it is the
method of alternating projections, established for two subspaces by von
Neumann \citeyearpar{vonneumann1950} and for arbitrarily many by
\citet{halperin1962}, with the Kaczmarz sweep \citep{kaczmarz1937} as
the coordinate special case (see \citealp{escalante2011} for a
monograph treatment and \citealp{deutschhundal1997} for rates), and it
was brought into fixed-effects estimation on exactly this foundation by
\citet{gaure2013}.  The \emph{combined} nonlinear cycle---profile
updates of the absorbed effects alternated with Newton/scoring steps on
a GLM likelihood---has, by contrast, been validated by simulation and
practice \citep{guimaraes2010,berge2018,correia2020} rather than by
proof.  The following theorem supplies the missing unified statement
for the combined inner--outer iteration; its proof, and the finer rate
and asymptotic theory, are in the online supplement.

\begin{theorem}[Global convergence of the absorbed-factor iteration]
\label{thm:global-main}
Suppose the link is canonical, or more generally log-concave in the sense
that each per-observation contribution $\eta \mapsto \ell_i(\eta)$ is
strictly concave; suppose the maximum-likelihood estimate exists (no
separation, which the drop rule below enforces for the absorbed blocks);
and suppose the non-absorbed information block is nonsingular on the
initial superlevel set.  Then the iterates of the inner--outer scheme
above---closed-form or exact one-dimensional block maximizations of the
absorbed effects, alternated with Armijo-damped tilted-moment
Newton/scoring steps for $(\bgamma,\bdelta)$---increase the likelihood
monotonically and converge to the maximum-likelihood estimate, from any
starting value, for any (essentially) cyclic ordering of the blocks.
\end{theorem}

\noindent The proof (Supplement Theorem~S1) is a block-coordinate-ascent
argument on the concave profile objective, handling the parameter
space's flat directions and the inexact (damped-Newton) outer block; the
log-concave-link generality covers gamma--log and probit as well as the
canonical families.  The supplement further gives the \emph{local rate}
(Theorems~S2--S3): for two absorbed factors the per-sweep contraction
equals $\cos^2$ of the Friedrichs angle between the weighted factor
subspaces---the squared second singular value of a normalized weighted
cross-tabulation---and for $G \ge 3$ it is the spectral radius of a
level-space sweep operator built from the pairwise cross-tabulations
alone, computable by power iteration before touching the microdata, with
an angle-based product certificate; observed contraction factors match
these predictions to three decimals.  Because the estimator \emph{is} the
MLE, its asymptotics are the MLE's: classical at fixed dimension, and,
in the proportional regime of one Poisson fixed effect per bounded-size
cell ($J/N \to c \in (0,1)$), unbiased with valid cell-clustered standard
errors, because profiling then coincides with conditioning
(Theorems~S4--S6; cf.\ the incidental-parameter analysis of
\citealp{fernandezval2016}).  Separated absorbed groups (no events) are detected
and dropped a priori---the level-wise instance of the drop rule of
\citet{correia2019}, whose linear-programming certificate treats
separation in full generality and is implemented in
\texttt{ppmlhdfe} \citep{correia2020};
Section~\ref{sec:app-register} exercises the full composition at scale,
and Section~\ref{sec:app-flights} checks it against \texttt{pyfixest} on
real data.

\subsection{Sensitivity: imbalance and rare events}
\label{sec:sim-sensitivity}

Study~1f re-runs the exactness comparison under stress: cell sizes drawn
from a Zipf($1.6$) law (a few giant cells, a long tail of tiny ones) and
event rates pushed to $\sim\!0.2\%$ (Poisson means $\sim\!0.002$; logistic
prevalence $\sim\!0.5\%$)---the regime where approximate methods resort
to case-control subsampling \citep{fithian2014}---with event-free cells
dropped as the MLE requires.  Across all eight family $\times$ scenario combinations the
maximum deviation from dense Newton stays at $10^{-15}$--$10^{-14}$,
convergence takes at most $12$ iterations (rare-event fits need a few
more steps, as their likelihoods are flatter), and the streaming pass
time is unchanged: the accumulation is a data-independent $O(N)$ sweep,
so imbalance redistributes work across cells without changing its total.
Exactness is an algebraic identity, so it is expected to survive stress;
the study confirms that the \emph{implementation}---binned accumulation,
line search, drop rules---inherits it.

\subsection{Numerical stability}\label{sec:sim-stability}

Because the tilted iteration is the same Newton/Fisher iteration as the
full-design fit (Theorem~\ref{thm:tilted}), the question a numerical
analyst asks is whether the \emph{implementation}---binned accumulation,
log-domain likelihood, line search, pseudoinverse fallback---inherits the
conditioning of the underlying problem or degrades it.  Study~1g
(Table~\ref{tab:stability}) answers this on a battery of adversarial
scenarios at $N = 10^5$: near-collinear covariates (correlation
$0.9999$); an ill-scaled covariate (multiplied by $10^6$, giving
information-matrix condition number $\sim\!10^{17}$); extreme offsets
spanning $30$ log units; linear predictors driven to $\pm 40$
(over/underflow territory); Zipf($1.3$)-unbalanced cells; and an
informative covariate carried on a $10^8$ pedestal (catastrophic
cancellation in any naive sum of squares).  For each I report the
condition number, the deviation from dense Newton on the same data, and
the deviation from a cancellation-free reference---the mathematically
identical model with the continuous columns standardized, then mapped
back---so that a genuine conditioning failure is separated from a faithful
reproduction of a hard problem.

\subsection{On-time performance of NYC flights}\label{sec:app-flights}

The first application is to public data, chosen so that every number can
be reproduced from a fresh download: the \texttt{nycflights13} data set
\citep{wickham2018}---all $336{,}776$ flights departing New York City's
three airports in 2013, from the U.S.\ Bureau of Transportation
Statistics.  I model the probability of a late arrival (arrival delay
$\ge 15$ minutes, the FAA on-time criterion; $24.5\%$ of the $327{,}346$
flights with an observed delay) by logistic regression with a cell
structure of carrier $\times$ origin $\times$ destination $\times$
month---$J = 3{,}863$ observed combinations, entering through a
main-effects cell-level design with $k = 131$ parameters ($16$ carriers,
$3$ origins, $103$ destinations, $12$ months)---and $p = 2$ continuous
covariates: scheduled departure time (in hours, centered) and log
distance (standardized).  One destination (LEX, a single on-time
flight) is quasi-separated---its dummy's MLE is $-\infty$---and is
dropped by the level-wise separation rule, after which the MLE exists.

The tilted logistic fit converges in $7$ Newton iterations,
$1.3$ seconds including robust standard errors.  The diurnal effect is
the substantive headline: the odds of a late arrival multiply by
$1.11$ per hour of scheduled departure time (robust SE on the log scale
$0.001$)---delays cascade through the day, a finding familiar from the
air-transportation literature, here estimated jointly with a full
operational cell structure.  Log distance illustrates the boundary case
flagged in Section~\ref{sec:tilted-main}: distance is constant within
origin--destination routes, hence within cells, so after destination
main effects it is identified only through cross-route contrasts and is
correspondingly imprecise ($+0.65$, robust SE $0.28$)---the estimator
handles cell-constant continuous covariates correctly, and the standard
error reports how little such a covariate adds.

As external validation, the identical model fitted with
\texttt{statsmodels} GLM on the dense $327{,}345 \times 133$ design
($0.35$\,GB) gives a maximum absolute coefficient deviation of
$2.3 \times 10^{-13}$, at $19$ seconds versus $1.3$: the same estimator,
$15\times$ cheaper, with no design matrix in memory.

A second model on the same data exercises the high-dimensional
absorption path against \texttt{pyfixest}.  I now absorb
\emph{aircraft}---the $3{,}692$ tail numbers that survive the separation
drop---crossed with a twelve-level month cell structure (an aircraft
flies across months, so the two factors are identified), keeping the two
continuous covariates.  The tilted logistic fit with absorbed aircraft
converges in $31$ iterations and $1.5$ seconds; \texttt{pyfixest}'s
\texttt{feglm} on the same specification takes $2.1$ seconds, and the two
agree on the continuous coefficients to $1.2 \times 10^{-9}$ (departure
hour $0.103994$ from both).  This is the most stringent external check
I report: on real high-dimensional data, the tilted absorption reproduces
the estimate of a mature, purpose-built HDFE--GLM package to nine digits,
at comparable speed, while also carrying the cell-structured part of the
model that \texttt{pyfixest} treats as ordinary covariates
(script \texttt{app\_flights.py}; the data file is fetched from the
public repository cited in the script).

\subsection{A register-scale stress test}\label{sec:app-register}

The second case study is a full-composition benchmark at register
scale, on synthetic data with a known generating process so that
recovery can be verified exactly.  The rate structure is calibrated to
realistic all-cause mortality gradients (a Gompertz age slope,
sex and education log-rate contrasts of $0.1$--$0.45$, a secular trend),
the generator is 40 lines of the code supplement, and every number below
regenerates end to end in under a minute.  The configuration exercises
every element of Sections~\ref{sec:tilted}--\ref{sec:algorithms}
simultaneously: absorbed factors, cell-level design, continuous
covariates, separation handling, and cluster-robust inference.

The data comprise $N = 5{,}000{,}000$ person-year episodes ($35{,}526$
deaths; crude rate $9.5$ per $1{,}000$ person-years) with:
municipality $\times$ year fixed effects ($500 \times 20 = 10{,}000$
groups) absorbed by the profile Gauss--Seidel scheme of Section~\ref{sec:absorb-impl} (Algorithm~S1); a cell-level design of
education (4) $\times$ sex (2) $\times$ age band (12), $J = 96$ cells,
$k = 16$ parameters; and $p = 2$ continuous covariates---standardized log
income and a standardized pollution-exposure index correlated with the
municipality effects (making the fixed effects non-ignorable for it).
Standard errors are clustered on the $500$ municipalities.

The fit: $322$ zero-event municipality--year groups ($159{,}464$
episodes) are dropped by the separation rule of Section~\ref{sec:absorb-impl}; the remaining model---%
$9{,}678$ absorbed effects, $16$ cell-level and $2$ individual-level
coefficients---converges in $8$ tilted Newton iterations,
$11.2$ seconds end to end on one core.  A dense dummy-variable design for
the same model would occupy $N \times 9{,}696$ doubles $\approx 0.4$\,TB
before the first iteration.  For verification at feasible scale, the
subpopulation of the first $10$ municipalities ($n = 97{,}723$, $196$
absorbed groups) was fitted both ways: maximum absolute coefficient
deviation $4.0 \times 10^{-11}$, tilted $0.1$s versus dense $3.0$s.

Substantively, the model recovers the calibrated truth within sampling
error: income $-0.180$ (cluster SE $0.005$; truth $-0.18$), pollution
$+0.049$ (SE $0.005$; truth $+0.06$, within two SEs, identified from
within-municipality variation only, as it must be after absorption),
female $-0.455$ (truth $-0.45$), education gradients $-0.13/-0.27/-0.40$
(truth $-0.12/-0.25/-0.40$), and a monotone age gradient tracking the
Gompertz truth to two decimals.  The point of the exercise is not these
numbers---the generator made them true---but the computational
demonstration: a model of this shape, with inference, is an
interactive-latency computation under the tilted-moment organization,
whereas a dense dummy-variable design for the same model would need to
allocate a terabyte-scale matrix before its first iteration.

\section{Discussion}\label{sec:discussion}
% =========================================================================

I have established a single algorithmic result and measured its
consequences.  For any exponential dispersion family whose design mixes
cell-constant categorical structure with continuous covariates, the
score vector and information matrix at an arbitrary parameter
value---and with them exact Newton and Fisher-scoring steps, profile
Hessians, and sandwich and cluster-robust covariance matrices---are
linear combinations of three tilted within-cell moments that one
streaming $O(N(p^2+p+1))$ pass produces
(Theorem~\ref{thm:tilted}, Corollary~\ref{cor:complexity}).  The
likelihood \emph{function} admits no such reduction
(Proposition~\ref{prop:sufficiency}); the iteration does, and the gap
between those two statements is the method.  The experiments bound its
value against each relevant alternative: exactness against dense Newton
at $10^{-13}$--$10^{-15}$; cost and memory against the strongest
general-purpose competitor (sparse-design IRLS) and against production
solvers; $O(N)$ scaling to $5\times10^7$ observations; and accuracy
across a stability battery reaching condition numbers of $10^{17}$.
Because the accumulators are additive across data partitions, the pass
extends directly to distributed and out-of-core execution; a tuned
(threaded, cache-blocked, or GPU segmented-reduction) implementation of
the accumulation kernel is the natural engineering continuation.

High-dimensional fixed-effect absorption served as the stress test, and
the division of labor bears repeating: absorbed factors handle dummy
structure too large even for cells, the tilted pass handles everything
else, and neither ever enters a design matrix.  The inner demeaning
loop's convergence is classical alternating-projections theory
\citep{vonneumann1950,halperin1962,gaure2013}; the contribution here is
the unified guarantee for the combined nonlinear cycle
(Theorem~\ref{thm:global-main}) and, in the supplement, its local rates
from pairwise cross-tabulations, asymptotics up to the proportional
regime $J/N \to c$, and Firth and penalized variants computed inside
the same pass.  These results are kept out of the main text so that the
tilting engine stands alone as the subject.

\paragraph{Software.}  All results regenerate from a self-contained
open-source Python/NumPy reference implementation
(\texttt{tilted\_glm.py}, roughly $800$ lines) released with the
manuscript and organized so that the correspondence between code and
stated results is line-by-line auditable; the benchmark harness fits
every competitor in an isolated subprocess with peak-memory accounting.
A companion Stata command (\texttt{stpois}), distributed in the same
repository, implements the Poisson event-history case for applied use.
Both are available at \url{https://github.com/andreasljungstrm/stpois}.

\subsection{Proof of Proposition~\ref{prop:sufficiency}}

(i) If $(\bx_i, o_i) = (\bar\bx_j, \bar o_j)$ within cells, then
$\eta_i = \bgamma'\bar\bx_j + \bdelta'\bw_j + \bar o_j$ is constant
within cells and \eqref{eq:loglik} becomes
$\sum_j \{ (\bgamma'\bar\bx_j + \bdelta'\bw_j + \bar o_j) D_j
- n_j\, b(\bgamma'\bar\bx_j + \bdelta'\bw_j + \bar o_j)\}$ up to
constants, a function of the displayed cell statistics for any cumulant
$b$; the collapsed likelihood is the microdata likelihood up to an
additive constant, so their maximizers and Hessians coincide.  For the
Poisson family the exposure offset need not be constant, since
$\sum_{i \in j} e^{\eta_i} = T_j\, e^{\bgamma'\bar\bx_j +
\bdelta'\bw_j}$ collects the offsets into $T_j$.

(ii) Writing $s_j = \bdelta'\bw_j$, the log likelihood is, up to
constants,
\[
\ell(\bgamma, \bdelta)
= \sum_j \Big\{ \bgamma' S_j + s_j D_j + \textstyle\sum_{i\in j} o_i y_i
   - G_j(s_j, \bgamma) \Big\},
\qquad
G_j(s, \bgamma) = \sum_{i \in j} b(s + \bgamma'\bx_i + o_i),
\]
which establishes computability from $\{S_j, D_j, G_j\}$.  Conversely,
suppose a statistic permits evaluation of $\ell$ on an open set
$\mathcal{O} \ni (\bgamma_0, \bdelta_0)$.  Provided the map $j \mapsto
\bw_j$ is injective (cells are distinct covariate combinations) and
$\bdelta \mapsto (s_1, \dots, s_J)$ has open range on $\mathcal{O}$'s
$\bdelta$-slice---as holds whenever the $\bw_j$ are affinely
independent, and in general after passing to the identified
subfamily---one recovers each $G_j(\cdot, \bgamma)$ on an open interval
of $s$ for each $\bgamma$ in the slice.  Each $G_j(\cdot, \bgamma)$ is a
finite sum of translates of the real-analytic $b$, hence real-analytic
on $\R$; knowledge on an open interval therefore determines it on the
whole line by analytic continuation.  The boundary hypothesis
$b(u) = e^u(1 + o(1))$ as $u \to -\infty$ then gives
\[
e^{-s}\, G_j(s, \bgamma)
= \sum_{i \in j} e^{-s}\, b(s + \bgamma'\bx_i + o_i)
\;\longrightarrow\; \sum_{i \in j} e^{\bgamma'\bx_i + o_i}
= \mathcal{L}_{\nu_j}(\bgamma),
\qquad s \to -\infty,
\]
termwise (for Poisson the relation is exact at every $s$; for Bernoulli
it is the expansion $\log(1 + \varepsilon) = \varepsilon(1 + o(1))$).
Hence the statistic determines $\mathcal{L}_{\nu_j}$ on an open subset
of $\R^p$; $\mathcal{L}_{\nu_j}$ is entire because $\nu_j$ is finite and
discrete, so it is determined everywhere by analytic continuation, and
the Laplace transform of a finite positive measure determines the
measure.  The statistic therefore determines $\nu_j$, whose minimal
description has dimension equal to (one plus $p$ times) its support
size.

(iii) Jensen's inequality applied to the strictly convex map
$z \mapsto e^z$ under the probability measure $\nu_j / \nu_j(\R^p)$
gives $\nu_j(\R^p)^{-1}\mathcal{L}_{\nu_j}(\bgamma) \ge
\exp(\bgamma' \bar\bx_j)$ with equality iff $\bgamma'\bx$ is
$\nu_j$-a.s.\ constant. \qed

\subsection{Proof of Theorem~\ref{thm:tilted}}

Differentiating \eqref{eq:loglik} with $\theta_i = \eta_i$:
$\phi\,\partial\ell/\partial\bgamma = \sum_i (y_i - b'(\eta_i))\bx_i$ and
$\phi\,\partial\ell/\partial\bdelta = \sum_i (y_i - b'(\eta_i))
\bw_{j(i)}$.  Grouping the second sum by cells and using that $\bw_j$ is
constant within cell $j$ gives $\sum_j \bw_j R_j$; grouping the first
gives $\sum_i y_i \bx_i - \sum_j \mathbf{m}^{b'}_j$ with
$\mathbf{m}^{b'}_j = \sum_{i\in j} b'(\eta_i)\bx_i = B_j
\E_{P^{b'}_{j,\btheta}}[\bx]$ by the definition of the $b'$-tilt.
Differentiating again,
$\phi\,\bF = \sum_i b''(\eta_i)\, \ba_i \ba_i'$ with $\ba_i =
(\bx_i', \bw_{j(i)}')'$; expanding the blocks and grouping by cells
yields (ii), and the moment identities follow from
Definition~\ref{def:tilt}.  For (iii), $b' = b'' = \exp$ gives
$\omega_i = e^{\bdelta'\bw_j}\, t_i e^{\bgamma'\bx_i}$, the cell factor
cancels in the normalization, and $M_j^{(0)} = e^{\bdelta'\bw_j}
\mathcal{L}_{\nu_j}(\bgamma)$; the cumulant identities
$\E_{P_{j,\bgamma}}[\bx] = \nabla K_j$ and $\Var = \nabla^2 K_j$ are the
standard derivatives of a log-Laplace transform, and
$\E[\bx\bx'] = \Var + \E\E'$ gives the stated $\bgamma$-block.  (iv) The
score and information assembled from (i)--(ii) are, entry for entry, the
same matrices as those computed from the stacked design; hence so is the
Newton step. \qed

\subsection{Proof of Corollary~\ref{cor:complexity}}

The microdata pass evaluates, per observation, $\eta_i$ from the cached
cell linear predictor plus $\bgamma'\bx_i$ ($O(p)$), the scalars
$b'(\eta_i), b''(\eta_i)$ ($O(1)$), the binned accumulations of
$\omega_i$ and $\omega_i \bx_i$ into cell $j(i)$ ($O(p)$), and the
symmetric rank-one update $\omega_i \bx_i \bx_i'$ ($O(p^2)$ scalar
multiply--adds, $p(p+1)/2$ distinct); total $O(N(p^2 + p + 1))$.
Assembly of $\sum_j M_j^{(0)}\bw_j\bw_j'$ and $\sum_j \mathbf{m}_j\bw_j'$
costs $O(J(k^2 + kp))$, and the symmetric solve $O((p+k)^3)$.  The dense
iteration forms $\sum_i \omega_i \ba_i\ba_i'$ at $O(N(p+k)^2)$ plus the
same solve.  Ratios follow.  With $p = 0$ the pass reduces to two binned
sums ($R_j$, $M_j^{(0)}$), and if the offsets enter through cell sums
(pure rate models), even these are fixed after one pass. \qed

\subsection{Proof of Proposition~\ref{prop:noncanonical}}

The ML score for a general link is $\sum_i (y_i - \mu_i)
\frac{\partial \mu_i / \partial \eta_i}{V(\mu_i)}\, \ba_i
= \sum_i (y_i - \mu_i)\, g'(\mu_i)\, \omega_i\, \ba_i = \sum_i r_i
\ba_i$, using $\partial\mu/\partial\eta = 1/g'(\mu)$, and the expected
information is $\sum_i \omega_i \ba_i \ba_i'$
\citep[Sec.~2.5]{mccullagh1989}.  These have the same
cell-decomposable form as in Theorem~\ref{thm:tilted} with
$(\omega_i, r_i)$ in place of $(b''(\eta_i), y_i - b'(\eta_i))$, so the
accumulation and complexity claims carry over.  A fixed point of the
scoring iteration has $\sum_i r_i \ba_i = \bzero$, i.e., is a root of
the ML score equations.  For the gamma--log pair, $V(\mu) = \mu^2$ and
$g'(\mu) = 1/\mu$ give $\omega_i = 1/(\mu_i^2 \mu_i^{-2}) = 1$; the
moments \eqref{eq:moments} are then constant in $\btheta$, and only
$R_j = \sum_{i\in j}(y_i - \mu_i)/\mu_i$ requires re-evaluation, an
$O(Np)$ pass. \qed
